## 1. Introduction to FDA Diversity Requirements

FDA diversity requirements for clinical trials in the United States have shifted from long standing guidance based expectations to a more formalized mandate structure that is now anchored in FDORA. As of March 10, 2026, sponsors operating in North America must plan for FDA scrutiny of whether pivotal evidence reflects the intended use population, with particular attention to historically underrepresented groups and to standardized demographic data collection.

This section establishes the baseline concepts used throughout the report.
- FDA expectations now combine statutory requirements for Diversity Action Plans with guidance on trial design and enrollment practices intended to broaden access and improve representativeness.
- The policy rationale is both scientific and ethical, focused on generalizability of evidence, detection of clinically meaningful subgroup differences, and equitable access to research participation.
- The practical implication is that diversity planning is increasingly treated as a core element of development strategy rather than a late stage recruitment activity.

### 1.1. Contextual background on current FDA diversity expectations in clinical trials

Current expectations and where they come from.
- Statutory expectations: FDORA created explicit requirements for Diversity Action Plans for certain clinical studies supporting drugs and devices, embedding diversity planning into the regulatory process rather than leaving it solely to sponsor discretion [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Guidance based expectations: FDA finalized guidance in December 2025 on enhancing participation through eligibility criteria, enrollment practices, and trial designs, positioning representativeness as a design and operations issue, not only a recruitment issue [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Data standardization expectations: FDA issued updated recommendations on collecting and reporting race and ethnicity data, revising its 2016 approach and emphasizing more consistent demographic data practices in submissions [3](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/collection-race-and-ethnicity-data-clinical-trials-and-clinical-studies-fda-regulated-medical).

What FDA is trying to achieve.
- Evidence generalizability: FDA frames diversity as necessary to strengthen the applicability of clinical evidence to the patients likely to use the product, especially when disease burden and treatment response may vary across populations [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Detection of subgroup differences: More representative enrollment increases the likelihood of identifying clinically relevant differences in safety, effectiveness, and dosing across demographic subgroups [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Transparency and accountability: Standardized demographic data collection and reporting supports clearer interpretation of who was studied and where evidence gaps remain [3](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/collection-race-and-ethnicity-data-clinical-trials-and-clinical-studies-fda-regulated-medical).

Scientific and ethical implications for sponsors and regulators.
- Scientific validity: Underrepresentation can reduce external validity and can mask heterogeneity of treatment effect, increasing uncertainty at approval and postmarket stages [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Ethical conduct: FDA and HHS framing links diversity to fairness in access to research participation and to avoiding systematic exclusion through overly restrictive eligibility criteria [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Operational accountability: Diversity planning is increasingly expected to be documented, justified, and reviewable, including rationale for enrollment goals and methods to achieve them [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).

Interpretation for North American government officials.
- Federal regulators are moving from encouragement to structured expectations, which increases the need for clear implementation guidance, measurable objectives, and realistic compliance timelines.
- Because many pivotal programs are multinational, FDA expectations can influence enrollment strategies and site selection across Canada and the United States when sponsors seek US approval [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).

### 1.2. Evolution of diversity mandates under FDORA and recent policy shifts

How mandates evolved into the current framework.
- Pre FDORA foundation: FDA issued final guidance in 2020 on enhancing diversity through eligibility criteria, enrollment practices, and trial designs, establishing a baseline expectation that sponsors should proactively reduce unnecessary exclusions and improve access [4](https://www.fda.gov/science-research/clinical-trials-and-human-subject-protection/clinical-trials-guidance-documents).
- Early planning emphasis: FDA released a draft diversity plans guidance in April 2022 focused on improving enrollment of underrepresented racial and ethnic populations, signaling a shift toward formal planning even before FDORA was enacted [5](https://www.fda.gov/media/184768/download).
- Statutory mandate: FDORA added new statutory provisions requiring Diversity Action Plans for certain phase 3 and other pivotal studies for drugs and certain device studies, with limited waiver pathways [6](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCODE-2023-title21/pdf/USCODE-2023-title21-chap9.pdf).
- Implementation guidance: FDA issued a more detailed draft guidance in June 2024 describing the form, content, timing, and waiver process for Diversity Action Plans, explicitly replacing the April 2022 draft [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Design and operations reinforcement: FDA finalized updated guidance in December 2025 on enhancing participation, reinforcing that diversity is driven by protocol design choices and operational practices, not only outreach [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).

Timeline of key shifts and practical impacts.
- 2020: FDA final guidance established recommended approaches such as broadening eligibility criteria, selecting sites that reflect the intended population, and reducing participant burden [4](https://www.fda.gov/science-research/clinical-trials-and-human-subject-protection/clinical-trials-guidance-documents).
- 2022: FDA draft diversity plans guidance introduced structured planning concepts focused on underrepresented racial and ethnic populations, increasing sponsor expectations for proactive enrollment planning [5](https://www.fda.gov/media/184768/download).
- 2022: FDORA enacted statutory Diversity Action Plan requirements for certain pivotal studies, creating a compliance oriented planning obligation and a waiver concept tied to impracticability or public health emergency needs [6](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCODE-2023-title21/pdf/USCODE-2023-title21-chap9.pdf).
- 2024: FDA draft Diversity Action Plans guidance expanded detail on enrollment goals, rationale, and operational measures, and described how FDA would evaluate waiver requests [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- 2025: FDA final enhancing participation guidance updated and consolidated recommendations, aligning diversity expectations with contemporary trial models and operational practices [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).

What changed under FDORA in practical terms.
- From best practice to required artifact: Diversity planning moved from recommended approaches to a required plan for specified studies, increasing documentation and review touchpoints [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- From single dimension to multi attribute goals: The Diversity Action Plans draft guidance describes enrollment goals by age group, sex, race, and ethnicity, expanding beyond earlier emphasis that was often interpreted as primarily race and ethnicity focused [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Waiver concept formalized: FDORA provides for waivers in limited circumstances, which shifts sponsor strategy toward early feasibility assessment and early engagement with FDA when constraints exist [6](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCODE-2023-title21/pdf/USCODE-2023-title21-chap9.pdf).

Current state as of March 10, 2026.
- FDA Diversity Action Plans guidance remains in draft form on FDA website as of the most recent FDA posting available in the sources reviewed, so sponsors are operating with statutory requirements plus draft implementation recommendations [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- FDA has finalized the broader enhancing participation guidance in December 2025, which provides current final recommendations on design and enrollment practices that support diversity objectives [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).

### 1.3. Importance of inclusive trial populations for regulatory and scientific validity

Why inclusivity is treated as a validity issue, not only an equity issue.
- Regulatory decision quality: FDA approval decisions rely on whether evidence supports safety and effectiveness for the intended use population, and unrepresentative enrollment can increase uncertainty about real world performance [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Subgroup assessment: Inclusive enrollment improves the ability to evaluate clinically meaningful differences across demographic subgroups, including potential differences in benefit risk profile [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Data interpretability: Standardized collection and reporting of race and ethnicity data improves interpretability of trial results and comparability across studies and submissions [3](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/collection-race-and-ethnicity-data-clinical-trials-and-clinical-studies-fda-regulated-medical).

Scientific mechanisms through which diversity improves evidence.
- External validity: Broader representation reduces the risk that trial results reflect a narrow subset of patients who differ from the intended use population in comorbidities, access patterns, or baseline risk [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Heterogeneity of treatment effect: More diverse enrollment increases the chance of detecting differential response or safety signals that may correlate with demographic factors or with correlated social and clinical determinants [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Measurement quality: Consistent demographic data collection supports more reliable subgroup analyses and postmarket evidence planning when premarket enrollment is constrained [3](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/collection-race-and-ethnicity-data-clinical-trials-and-clinical-studies-fda-regulated-medical).

Ethical and public trust dimensions relevant to government officials.
- Fair access: FDA guidance emphasizes reducing unnecessary participant burden and removing avoidable exclusions, which aligns with ethical principles of justice in research participation [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Community confidence: Diversity Action Plans encourage intentional engagement and retention strategies, which can support trust and sustained participation among historically underrepresented communities [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).

What inclusive validity means for evidence packages submitted to FDA.
- Enrollment goals and rationale: Diversity Action Plans are expected to articulate enrollment goals and the rationale for those goals, linking them to disease epidemiology and intended use population considerations [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Design choices as validity levers: FDA final guidance highlights eligibility criteria, site selection, and participant burden reduction as primary levers that affect who can enroll and remain in a study [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Demographic data quality: FDA demographic data guidance supports consistent reporting in regulatory submissions, enabling regulators to assess representativeness and interpret subgroup evidence more reliably [3](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/collection-race-and-ethnicity-data-clinical-trials-and-clinical-studies-fda-regulated-medical).

Bottom line for this report.
- FDA diversity requirements should be treated as an evidence quality and regulatory readiness issue with direct implications for development timelines, feasibility, and downstream health outcomes.
- The collision with enrollment economics arises because representativeness often requires additional sites, expanded outreach, and participant support, which this report addresses in later sections.

## 2. Historical Development of FDA Diversity Guidelines

FDA diversity expectations evolved from early, largely nonbinding recommendations focused on protecting potentially vulnerable groups, toward a modern framework that treats representativeness as a scientific validity requirement and, since FDORA, a pre specified planning obligation. The historical arc is marked by three reinforcing forces: changes in ethical norms about participant autonomy, statutory and regulatory requirements for demographic data collection and analysis, and periodic policy pushes that increased transparency and operational accountability for sponsors.

Across this evolution, a recurring implementation gap persisted: guidance and reporting requirements improved demographic visibility, but did not consistently change enrollment behavior until diversity planning became tied to pivotal study governance and submission processes. This history explains why current mandates emphasize early protocol level planning, measurable enrollment goals, and waiver criteria rather than late stage recruitment fixes.

### 2.1. Early diversity initiatives and their limitations in trial design

Key early milestones that shaped FDA thinking.
- 1977 guidance and protective exclusion: FDA guidance on clinical evaluation of drugs recommended excluding women of childbearing potential from early phase studies until reproductive toxicity data were available, reflecting a protection oriented posture that reduced generalizability for women and constrained early dose finding evidence. This approach is summarized in National Academies background material describing the 1977 guidance and its practical effect on enrollment[7](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK236532/) .
- 1993 pivot toward inclusion and sex based evaluation: FDA issued the Guideline for the Study and Evaluation of Gender Differences in the Clinical Evaluation of Drugs as a Federal Register notice dated July 22, 1993, encouraging analysis of sex differences and supporting broader inclusion of women in clinical development[8](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/notice-files/not93-224.html) .
- 1994 institutionalization of women focused regulatory science: FDA established the Office of Women’s Health in 1994 by congressional mandate, strengthening internal capacity to promote inclusion and sex differences research relevant to regulatory decisions[9](https://www.fda.gov/about-fda/office-commissioner/office-womens-health) .

Early initiatives that broadened diversity beyond sex.
- 1998 Demographic Rule and subgroup reporting expectations: FDA regulations for marketing applications require safety data to be presented by gender, age, and racial subgroups in specified contexts, embedding demographic subgroup analysis into submission expectations even when enrollment remained sponsor driven[10](https://codes.findlaw.com/cfr/title-21-food-and-drugs/cfr-sect-21-314-50/) .
- Standardization of race and ethnicity data collection: FDA guidance on Collection of Race and Ethnicity Data in Clinical Trials, finalized October 2016, recommended standardized categories aligned with federal data standards and supported consistent reporting in regulatory submissions[11](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/collection-race-and-ethnicity-data-clinical-trials) .
- Health equity infrastructure within FDA: FDA established the Office of Minority Health in 2010 and renamed it the Office of Minority Health and Health Equity in 2019, signaling a broader agency commitment to disparities and equity in medical product development and communication[12](https://www.fda.gov/consumers/minority-health-and-health-equity/fda-office-minority-health-and-health-equity-10-year-anniversary) . [13](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/fda-insight/fda-insight-minority-health-and-health-equity).

Why early initiatives had limited impact on trial design and enrollment economics.
- Nonbinding nature and weak operational hooks: Early FDA documents primarily encouraged inclusion and analysis but generally did not require prospective enrollment targets or enforceable recruitment plans, allowing sponsors to prioritize speed and site productivity over representativeness.
- Late stage visibility rather than early stage design control: Requirements to present subgroup data in submissions improved transparency at approval time, but did not necessarily change upstream eligibility criteria, site selection, or participant support budgets.
- Data standardization without participation guarantees: Standardized race and ethnicity collection improved comparability, but did not address structural barriers such as travel burden, language access, and mistrust that affect enrollment.

### 2.2. Legislative milestones including FDORA 2022 and related mandates

Legislation and congressional direction that progressively increased accountability.
- NIH Revitalization Act of 1993 as a parallel federal inclusion mandate: While not an FDA statute, the NIH Revitalization Act of 1993 required NIH to establish guidelines for inclusion of women and minorities in clinical research, shaping the broader US research ecosystem and expectations for representativeness that later influenced FDA policy discourse[14](https://orwh.od.nih.gov/sites/orwh/files/docs/NIH-Revitalization-Act-1993.pdf) . [15](https://grants.nih.gov/index.php/policy-and-compliance/policy-topics/inclusion/women-and-minorities/guideline).
- FDASIA 2012 Section 907 as a catalyst for demographic subgroup transparency: FDASIA directed FDA to evaluate inclusion and analysis of demographic subgroups and to develop an action plan to improve completeness, quality, and public availability of subgroup data[16](https://www.congress.gov/bill/112th-congress/senate-bill/3187/text/eah) . [17](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/food-and-drug-administration-safety-and-innovation-act-fdasia/fdasia-section-907-inclusion-demographic-subgroups-clinical-trials).
- FDA Action Plan 2014 as an implementation bridge: FDA’s August 2014 Action Plan described 27 actions organized around quality, participation, and transparency, explicitly linking demographic subgroup data practices to enrollment barriers and public reporting[18](https://www.fda.gov/media/89307/download) .
- Drug Trials Snapshots beginning 2015 as a transparency mechanism: CDER began publishing Drug Trials Snapshots for novel drugs approved beginning January 2015, providing public summaries of who participated in key trials and whether outcomes differed by demographic subgroups[19](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-approvals-and-databases/drug-trials-snapshots-summary-report-2015-and-2016) . [20](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-approvals-and-databases/drug-trials-snapshots).

FDORA 2022 as the turning point from encouragement to mandate.
- Statutory requirement for Diversity Action Plans: The Food and Drug Omnibus Reform Act of 2022 amended the FD and C Act to require diversity action plans for certain clinical studies, shifting diversity from a best practice to a required planning artifact tied to pivotal development. The FDA draft guidance page explicitly states it is mandated under section 3602 of FDORA and references sections 505(z) and 520(g) as amended by section 3601[1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies) .
- Evolution from April 2022 diversity plans draft to broader DAP framework: FDA issued an April 14, 2022 draft focused on underrepresented racial and ethnic populations, later replaced by a broader draft on Diversity Action Plans for underrepresented populations in clinical studies, reflecting expansion from race and ethnicity only toward multi dimensional underrepresentation[1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies) .

Policy significance for enrollment economics.
- Earlier statutes emphasized reporting and transparency, which increased reputational and review pressure but still allowed sponsors to treat diversity as a downstream recruitment problem.
- FDORA changed the economic calculus by requiring prospective goals, rationale, and operational approach, increasing the likelihood that diversity costs are budgeted earlier and managed as part of protocol feasibility and site strategy.

### 2.3. Shifts in FDA policy across administrations and their impact on diversity guidance

Administration linked shifts are best understood as changes in emphasis and implementation tools rather than changes in the underlying scientific rationale. Across administrations, FDA activity has oscillated between transparency focused approaches, guidance focused operational recommendations, and statutory implementation work once Congress created enforceable planning requirements.

Notable policy shifts and their practical impacts.
- 2012 to 2016 period emphasizing transparency and subgroup data quality: Following FDASIA 2012, FDA produced the 2014 Action Plan and expanded public reporting through Drug Trials Snapshots beginning in 2015, increasing external visibility of demographic composition and subgroup outcomes[18](https://www.fda.gov/media/89307/download) . [19](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-approvals-and-databases/drug-trials-snapshots-summary-report-2015-and-2016).
- 2019 to 2020 period emphasizing trial design levers: FDA issued draft guidance in 2019 and finalized guidance in November 2020 on enhancing diversity through eligibility criteria, enrollment practices, and trial designs, shifting attention from reporting to upstream design choices that affect feasibility and cost[21](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-participation-clinical-trials-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial-designs) .
- 2025 period emphasizing finalized cross cutting guidance: FDA issued a final guidance in December 2025 titled Enhancing Participation in Clinical Trials, reinforcing design and operational approaches to increase representativeness across demographic and non demographic characteristics[21](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-participation-clinical-trials-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial-designs) .
- Post FDORA implementation period emphasizing statutory compliance: FDA issued draft guidance on Diversity Action Plans describing form, content, timing, and waiver processes, explicitly tied to FDORA mandates, reflecting a shift from voluntary guidance to compliance oriented planning[1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies) .

How these shifts affected diversity guidance content.
- From who is enrolled to how enrollment is achieved: Earlier efforts focused on describing demographic composition and subgroup analyses, while later guidance emphasized operational tactics such as broadening eligibility, reducing participant burden, and improving site selection strategies.
- From retrospective reporting to prospective governance: The DAP framework requires sponsors to define enrollment goals and methods before pivotal studies, increasing the likelihood of early budget allocation for community engagement, decentralized elements, and participant support.
- From center specific initiatives to cross center alignment: FDA’s diversity related work increasingly appears as cross cutting guidance and agency wide initiatives, supported by offices focused on women’s health and minority health and health equity[9](https://www.fda.gov/about-fda/office-commissioner/office-womens-health) . [12](https://www.fda.gov/consumers/minority-health-and-health-equity/fda-office-minority-health-and-health-equity-10-year-anniversary).

## 3. Current FDA Regulatory Framework

As of March 10, 2026, the FDA diversity framework for clinical trials in the United States is best understood as a two track system. One track is nonbinding but highly influential guidance on trial design and conduct that is routinely referenced in FDA interactions and review. The second track is a statutory mandate created by FDORA that requires Diversity Action Plans for certain studies, with the operational trigger tied to FDA final guidance on the form and manner of submission, and with downstream transparency and reporting expectations embedded in statute. The December 2025 final guidance on enhancing participation is the most current FDA statement consolidating practical expectations on eligibility criteria, enrollment practices, and trial design to improve representativeness [22](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-participation-clinical-trials-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practactices-and-trial-designs).

A key implementation reality for sponsors is that FDA expectations now extend beyond retrospective demographic reporting. Sponsors are expected to prospectively design protocols and operational plans that reduce avoidable exclusions, expand access points, and support retention for populations historically underrepresented in clinical research, while maintaining participant safety and interpretability of results [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).

### 3.1. Overview of finalized guidances: eligibility criteria, enrollment practices, trial design (Dec 2025)

The FDA issued a final guidance in December 2025 titled Enhancing Participation in Clinical Trials Eligibility Criteria Enrollment Practices and Trial Designs. This document is a revision of earlier FDA thinking and is positioned as the Agency’s current recommendations for broadening participation through protocol design and operational choices, while preserving safety and scientific validity [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).

Eligibility criteria expectations.
- Fit for purpose criteria: Sponsors are expected to justify exclusions based on safety and scientific necessity rather than convenience, and to reassess legacy exclusions that disproportionately screen out older adults, people with comorbidities, and other underrepresented groups [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Broader clinical reality: FDA encourages criteria that better reflect real world use populations, including reconsideration of restrictive laboratory thresholds, organ function cutoffs, and comorbidity exclusions when not essential to risk control or endpoint interpretability [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Operationalizing inclusivity: The guidance emphasizes that eligibility decisions should be paired with operational mitigations such as enhanced safety monitoring, dose modification rules, and stratification plans rather than default exclusions [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).

Enrollment practices expectations.
- Community anchored recruitment: FDA highlights the need for recruitment approaches that reach underrepresented populations through trusted channels and reduce friction in screening and consent processes, including culturally and linguistically appropriate materials [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Access supports: The guidance discusses practical supports that can improve participation and retention, such as transportation support, visit flexibility, and use of local facilities for certain assessments when appropriate [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Site selection and investigator diversity: FDA points to the role of selecting sites that serve diverse communities and ensuring site staff are prepared to engage populations that have historically faced barriers to research participation [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).

Trial design expectations.
- Design choices that reduce burden: FDA encourages designs that reduce participant burden where feasible, including fewer in person visits, use of remote assessments, and hybrid approaches when they do not compromise data integrity [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Statistical planning for representativeness: Sponsors are expected to consider how enrollment patterns affect the ability to evaluate safety and effectiveness across relevant subgroups, including prespecification of subgroup analyses where appropriate [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Rare disease considerations: The guidance includes discussion of tailoring inclusivity strategies to rare diseases, where small populations and geographic dispersion require different operational approaches to broaden access without undermining feasibility [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).

### 3.2. Status of Diversity Action Plan (DAP) requirements under FDORA and guidance timeline

Statutory basis and scope.
- FDORA created a statutory requirement for certain sponsors to submit a Diversity Action Plan for applicable clinical studies for drugs, biological products, and devices. The statutory provisions are codified in the Federal Food Drug and Cosmetic Act and are reflected in the U.S. Code, including provisions addressing DAP content elements, waiver criteria, and FDA reporting on whether enrollment goals were met [24](https://www.govinfo.gov/link/uscode/21/355).
- The statute links the effective date of mandatory DAP submission to the publication of FDA final guidance specifying the form and manner of submission, with applicability tied to studies for which enrollment commences after the statutory post publication interval described in FDORA and reflected in legal analyses of the draft guidance timeline [25](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2024/07/fda-takes-next-step-in-advancing-diversity-in-clinical-studies).

Guidance status as of March 10, 2026.
- FDA issued a comprehensive draft guidance in June 2024 titled Diversity Action Plans to Improve Enrollment of Participants from Underrepresented Populations in Clinical Studies, with a public comment period that closed on September 26, 2024. Multiple legal and regulatory analyses note that FDORA directed FDA to finalize the guidance within a defined period after the comment close, and that the compliance trigger for mandatory DAP submission is tied to finalization and publication of that guidance [26](https://www.gibsondunn.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/fda-issues-overdue-guidance-on-diversity-action-plans-in-drug-and-device-clinical-trials-what-you-need-to-know.pdf).
- Public reporting and legal commentary in 2025 indicated uncertainty and delays around the DAP guidance finalization timeline, including periods when the draft guidance was reportedly not accessible on FDA’s website, creating operational ambiguity for sponsors planning pivotal trials. These reports did not change the underlying statutory mandate but did affect planning assumptions for when mandatory submission would be enforced in practice [27](https://www.dlapiper.com/en-us/insights/publications/2025/01/fda-actions-addressing-diversity-in-clinical-trials).

Sponsor expectations for compliance and innovation.
- Prospective planning: Even where final DAP guidance timing is uncertain, FDA’s direction of travel is clear that diversity planning should be integrated into protocol development, feasibility, and site strategy rather than treated as a late stage recruitment fix [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Study specific measures: The DAP concept is intended to focus on concrete enrollment and retention measures for the specific study, rather than general corporate initiatives, consistent with summaries of FDA messaging around the draft guidance [28](https://fda.complianceexpert.com/news/fda-provides-format-and-content-recommendations-for-trial-diversity-action-plans).
- Waivers and modifications: FDORA contemplates waiver criteria and modifications to DAPs, and directs FDA to address these topics in guidance, which sponsors should anticipate as part of protocol amendment governance and regulatory correspondence planning [24](https://www.govinfo.gov/link/uscode/21/355).

### 3.3. Enforcement mechanisms, compliance timelines, and documentation expectations

Regulatory leverage and enforcement mechanisms.
- Submission gating risk: Because FDORA makes DAP submission a statutory requirement for covered studies once the effective date is triggered, failure to submit a required DAP can create procedural risk at key regulatory touchpoints, including potential delays in study initiation interactions or downstream review timelines, as discussed in legal analyses of the draft guidance and statutory structure [24](https://www.govinfo.gov/link/uscode/21/355).
- Inspection and auditability: FDA can evaluate whether sponsors implemented enrollment and retention processes as described through routine oversight mechanisms, including bioresearch monitoring and inspectional review of trial conduct documentation. FDA’s broader clinical trials guidance ecosystem reinforces that sponsors should maintain documentation sufficient to demonstrate protocol compliance and operational execution [4](https://www.fda.gov/science-research/clinical-trials-and-human-subject-protection/clinical-trials-guidance-documents).
- Public accountability: FDORA includes provisions directing FDA to report in the aggregate on whether studies met demographic subgroup enrollment goals in submitted DAPs and reasons provided when goals were not met, increasing reputational and policy scrutiny even when enforcement is not framed as punitive [24](https://www.govinfo.gov/link/uscode/21/355).

Compliance timelines in practice.
- Effective date linkage: The mandatory DAP requirement is tied to enrollment commencement after the statutory interval following publication of final guidance on DAP submission form and manner. Sponsors should treat this as a hard operational dependency for pivotal program timelines and align protocol finalization and regulatory submission calendars accordingly [25](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2024/07/fda-takes-next-step-in-advancing-diversity-in-clinical-studies).
- Parallel expectation: Independently of DAP final guidance timing, FDA’s December 2025 final guidance sets current expectations for inclusive eligibility and participation enabling design, which FDA can reference during development discussions and review even when framed as nonbinding recommendations [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).

Documentation expectations for sponsors.
- Protocol level traceability: Maintain a clear rationale for inclusion and exclusion criteria, including documentation of revisions made to broaden eligibility and the safety monitoring or design features used to manage any incremental risk [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Enrollment operations evidence: Retain evidence of site selection rationale, community engagement activities, recruitment channel performance, and participant support measures that reduce access barriers, with version control and governance sign offs [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- DAP governance artifacts: Prepare controlled documents that capture enrollment goals, rationale for goals, planned enrollment and retention strategies, and processes for monitoring progress and implementing corrective actions, consistent with the statutory concept of a plan that can be assessed against outcomes and updated when needed [24](https://www.govinfo.gov/link/uscode/21/355).
- Progress and deviation management: Document monitoring outputs that compare actual enrollment to targets, root cause analyses when targets are missed, and protocol or operational amendments intended to improve representativeness without compromising data integrity [24](https://www.govinfo.gov/link/uscode/21/355).

## 4. Legal Implications of Non‑Compliance

Noncompliance risk has shifted from reputational and scientific critique to more direct regulatory exposure because FDORA created a statutory obligation to submit Diversity Action Plans for covered studies, while FDA continues to use established clinical trial oversight tools to address protocol and conduct deficiencies that can intersect with diversity planning. The most material legal risk for sponsors is not a single standalone penalty for low enrollment diversity, but a chain of consequences that can include FDA requests for protocol modification, clinical holds, refuse to file actions for incomplete marketing applications, postmarketing requirements to address evidence gaps, and follow on private litigation alleging misstatements, discrimination, or consumer and investor harm.

For North American government officials, the key policy implication is that enforcement leverage is strongest at submission and inspection touchpoints, while litigation risk is most acute when sponsors publicly represent compliance or representativeness and later face evidence gaps, safety signals, or disparate outcomes in underrepresented groups. Sponsors that treat diversity planning as a document only, rather than a governed operational control, face higher exposure across FDA compliance, product liability, securities disclosure, and federal funding compliance regimes.

### 4.1. Regulatory consequences and potential enforcement actions for sponsors

Primary FDA levers that can be triggered by diversity related noncompliance.

- Submission level completeness and reviewability risk.
- IND level oversight and clinical hold risk.
- Inspection findings and corrective action escalation.
- Approval and postapproval consequences when evidence is not generalizable.

How diversity requirements connect to these levers in practice.

- Diversity Action Plan submission failures: FDORA requires Diversity Action Plans for certain pivotal studies, and FDA guidance describes timing, content, waiver pathways, and evaluation approach. A missing or materially deficient plan can create regulatory friction at the study start and submission stages, especially when FDA expects the plan to be integrated with protocol level enrollment strategy and feasibility[1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies) .
- Protocol design and conduct deficiencies that undermine representativeness: FDA can request modifications or impose a hold when a protocol is clearly deficient in design to meet stated objectives or when conduct deviates from the investigational plan. Diversity related issues most often surface as feasibility failures, overly restrictive eligibility criteria, site selection choices, or operational barriers that make stated enrollment objectives unattainable, which can become part of broader protocol deficiency discussions[30](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/21/312.42) .
- Inspection and compliance escalation: FDA Bioresearch Monitoring inspections frequently focus on adherence to the investigational plan and data integrity. If diversity commitments are embedded in the protocol, monitoring plan, or sponsor oversight documentation, failure to execute can be framed as failure to follow the investigational plan, compounding exposure when combined with other GCP issues[34](https://www.fdli.org/2025/02/compliance-challenges-for-clinical-research-sites/) .
- Marketing application risk from evidence gaps: Even when a Diversity Action Plan exists, failure to enroll a population that supports generalizable safety and effectiveness can lead to FDA requests for additional analyses, additional studies, or postmarketing commitments, and can complicate labeling, risk management, and benefit risk determinations. FDA continues to emphasize trial designs and enrollment practices that improve participation and generalizability[2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial) .

Corrective measures FDA commonly expects when diversity execution fails.

- Root cause analysis tied to feasibility and site performance, including whether eligibility criteria, visit burden, language access, and participant support were realistically resourced.
- Protocol amendments that reduce avoidable exclusions and participant burden, with updated monitoring and recruitment controls.
- Statistical mitigation plans, including prespecified subgroup analyses where feasible and transparent limitations statements when representativeness is not achieved.
- Postmarketing evidence generation plans when premarket evidence is insufficient for key subgroups, aligned with FDA feedback and risk management needs.

Regulatory risk management implication for government officials.

- Enforcement is most effective when diversity planning is treated as a quality system element with auditable controls, rather than a narrative plan. Public filing checklists and established hold and refuse to file authorities increase the practical consequences of incomplete submissions and deficient planning[33](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-publishes-filing-checklists-prevent-submission-delays) .

### 4.2. Legal precedents and litigation related to diversity requirements in trials

The litigation landscape is more developed for adjacent theories than for direct lawsuits alleging failure to meet FDA diversity enrollment expectations. As of March 10, 2026, the most relevant precedents for sponsors arise from three litigation clusters.

- Administrative law challenges affecting diversity related federal actions.
- Securities and disclosure litigation tied to clinical trial representations.
- Civil rights and federal funding related fraud theories that can attach to diversity representations.

Administrative law and federal program litigation signals.

- Federal court scrutiny of DEI related agency actions: Litigation over federal agency DEI related decisions has produced court orders requiring restoration of certain NIH grants terminated under DEI related rationales, illustrating that diversity related policy actions can be litigated under administrative law theories and can generate rapid injunctive relief. While not FDA enforcement, it signals that diversity related government actions and sponsor reliance interests can become contested in court[35](https://www.axios.com/local/boston/2025/06/16/nih-grants-ordered-restored) .

Securities and disclosure litigation analogs.

- Clinical trial disclosure claims: Public companies have faced securities class actions alleging misrepresentations or omissions about clinical trial results and endpoints. Courts often focus on materiality, scienter, and whether statements were misleading in context. Diversity related claims are most plausible when sponsors make specific, measurable statements about representativeness, subgroup power, or regulatory readiness and later disclose shortfalls or FDA feedback[36](https://www.stblaw.com/about-us/publications/view/2023/05/01/fourth-circuit-affirms-dismissal-of-securities-fraud-class-action-alleging-misrepresentations-or-omissions-regarding-a-clinical-trial-drug-%28securities-law-alert%29) .

Civil rights and federal funding related fraud theories.

- False Claims Act risk via civil rights compliance certifications: DOJ has articulated a Civil Rights Fraud Initiative theory in which entities receiving federal funds may face False Claims Act exposure if they violate federal civil rights laws and falsely certify compliance in claims for payment. For sponsors and research institutions, this becomes relevant when federal grants, cooperative agreements, or reimbursement linked programs require certifications related to nondiscrimination or inclusion practices[37](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/public_contract_law/resources/procurement-lawyer/2025-fall/false-claims-act-risks-considerations-federal-funding-recipients/) .

Practical litigation risk scenarios for noncompliant sponsors.

- Product liability and failure to warn: Underrepresentation can be used by plaintiffs to argue inadequate testing in foreseeable user populations, especially if postmarket adverse events cluster in groups not adequately studied.
- Consumer protection and unfair practices: Marketing claims about broad applicability or equitable access can be challenged if trial evidence is not representative.
- Employment and contracting disputes: Diversity recruitment programs can create internal disputes if implemented without clear legal review, particularly where programs are perceived as using impermissible selection criteria.

Key takeaway for government officials.

- The most immediate legal precedent value is not a single landmark diversity enrollment case, but the way courts treat agency authority, sponsor disclosures, and certification based funding. These doctrines shape how aggressively sponsors will document, qualify, and govern diversity commitments.

### 4.3. Sponsor liability and risk management considerations

Core sponsor liability theories.

- Regulatory liability: Failure to submit required plans, failure to follow the investigational plan, and inadequate documentation can trigger FDA compliance actions, inspection findings, and submission delays[30](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/21/312.42) .
- Tort and product liability: Plaintiffs may argue that inadequate representation contributed to undetected safety risks or reduced effectiveness in specific populations, supporting claims of design defect, failure to warn, or negligent testing.
- Disclosure liability: Public statements about diversity goals, achieved enrollment, or generalizability can create securities and consumer protection exposure if later shown to be incomplete or misleading[36](https://www.stblaw.com/about-us/publications/view/2023/05/01/fourth-circuit-affirms-dismissal-of-securities-fraud-class-action-alleging-misrepresentations-or-omissions-regarding-a-clinical-trial-drug-%28securities-law-alert%29) .
- Funding and certification liability: Where federal funds are involved, inaccurate certifications related to nondiscrimination or program compliance can create False Claims Act risk under civil rights fraud theories[37](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/public_contract_law/resources/procurement-lawyer/2025-fall/false-claims-act-risks-considerations-federal-funding-recipients/) .

Risk management controls that reduce legal exposure.

- Governance and accountability.
- Treat Diversity Action Plans as controlled documents with versioning, approvals, and change control, aligned to protocol amendments and feasibility updates.
- Assign accountable owners across clinical operations, biostatistics, regulatory, and legal, with escalation triggers when enrollment diverges from targets.

- Documentation and audit readiness.
- Maintain contemporaneous evidence of rationale for enrollment goals, site selection decisions, eligibility criteria justifications, and participant support resourcing.
- Ensure monitoring plans include checks for demographic data completeness and protocol specified diversity related procedures.

- Contracting and vendor oversight.
- Embed diversity related performance obligations into CRO and site contracts, including data reporting cadence, community engagement deliverables, and corrective action timelines.
- Require vendors to document language access, transportation support, and decentralized visit capabilities where promised.

- Disclosure discipline.
- Use qualified, data backed language in investor and public communications, avoiding absolute claims about representativeness unless supported by locked datasets.
- Align external statements with FDA submissions and internal dashboards to reduce mismatch risk.

- Legal review of recruitment practices.
- Review recruitment materials and outreach strategies for compliance with federal and state nondiscrimination laws and privacy rules.
- Use inclusion focused access measures rather than impermissible exclusion or selection criteria, and document the scientific rationale for any subgroup focused enrollment caps or prioritization.

Implications for government officials designing policy.

- Policies that standardize documentation expectations and promote auditable controls can reduce both regulatory friction and litigation risk by making sponsor behavior more consistent and defensible.

Limitations and currency note.

- FDA has published the June 2024 draft Diversity Action Plan guidance and continues to emphasize diversity enhancing trial design in its December 2025 guidance on participation. As of March 10, 2026, publicly accessible FDA materials still present the Diversity Action Plan document as draft, and sponsors should monitor FDA updates for finalization and any changes to compliance timing[1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies) .

## 5. Economic Burden Analysis

This section translates FDA diversity expectations and FDORA Diversity Action Plan obligations into sponsor level budget impacts, focusing on incremental costs, operational friction, and the financial consequences of timeline risk. The core economic tension is that inclusive recruitment often increases near term cash outlays and execution complexity, while the financial penalty of delay can be material even when direct operating costs appear modest.

Sponsors should treat diversity execution as a portfolio of cost drivers that interact with each other. For example, expanding site geography can improve access but increases start up, monitoring, and vendor management load, which can then raise protocol deviation risk and amendment frequency if not paired with stronger training and data quality controls.

Interpretation for diversity planning.
- Budget owners should separate direct operating cost exposure from commercial opportunity cost exposure when diversity tactics increase the probability of timeline slip.

Key cost categories that recur across inclusive recruitment programs.
- Access enabling participant support: Transportation, lodging, meals, childcare, and flexible visit scheduling to reduce participation burden, consistent with FDA recommendations to improve access and reduce burden [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Language and cultural adaptation: Translation of materials, interpreter services, culturally competent staff training, and community partner engagement, aligned with FDA recommended enrollment practices [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Site network expansion and diversification: Adding community based sites, safety net systems, and nontraditional settings, which increases start up work, contracting, monitoring, and data management overhead.
- Screening and retention intensity: Higher touch outreach, more frequent reminders, and retention supports to reduce differential dropout that can erode subgroup interpretability.
- Data and reporting: More granular demographic data capture, monitoring, and reporting to support Diversity Action Plan execution and auditability expectations under FDORA aligned guidance processes [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).

Budget governance implications for North American public sector stakeholders.
- The economic burden is not limited to sponsor budgets because inclusive recruitment often depends on local clinical infrastructure, community organizations, and health system capacity that may be unevenly distributed.
- When sponsors rely on under resourced sites, additional funding for training, staffing, and technology may be required to avoid quality issues that can trigger rework and delay.

### 5.1. Cost implications of implementing inclusive recruitment strategies

Inclusive recruitment typically increases both fixed and variable costs.

Fixed cost increases that appear early in the program.
- Diversity Action Plan development and governance: Cross functional planning, feasibility work, and documentation to support the form and content expectations described in FDA Diversity Action Plan draft guidance, including rationale for enrollment goals and operational measures [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Protocol and materials redesign: Eligibility criteria review, visit schedule simplification, and participant facing material updates to reduce burden and improve access, consistent with FDA recommendations on eligibility and trial design [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Vendor and partner onboarding: Contracting with community organizations, patient navigation providers, translation vendors, and decentralized trial service providers.

Variable cost increases that scale with enrollment and geography.
- Participant support services: Reimbursement and direct provision of transportation and other supports to reduce practical barriers, which FDA explicitly identifies as a lever to improve participation [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).
- Expanded site footprint: More sites and more diverse sites can raise per patient overhead through additional monitoring visits, more contracts, more IRB coordination, and more site training.
- Higher screening effort: Underrepresented populations may require broader outreach channels and more intensive pre screening, increasing coordinator time and recruitment spend.

Cost offsets and cost avoidance mechanisms.
- Better feasibility and eligibility design can reduce screen failures and reduce the need for late site additions, which are often a major unplanned cost driver.
- Upfront investment in retention supports can reduce missing data and differential dropout that otherwise forces sample size increases or post hoc analyses that weaken regulatory confidence.

Practical budgeting guidance.
- Build an explicit diversity cost workstream budget line that includes participant support, translation, community engagement, and site enablement, rather than distributing these costs across generic recruitment and site management lines.
- Tie each cost item to a measurable operational objective such as reduced screen failure, reduced dropout, or faster time to first patient in, to support later ROI assessment.

### 5.2. Impact on trial timelines, resource allocation, and operational complexity

Diversity execution changes the critical path because it shifts work earlier and broadens the operational surface area.

Timeline effects that can extend study duration if not managed.
- Longer start up: Adding community based sites and nontraditional settings can increase contracting and activation time, especially when sites have limited research administration capacity.
- Slower enrollment ramp: New sites may require more training and operational stabilization before reaching steady state enrollment.
- Higher coordination load: More sites and more vendors increase the probability of operational friction, including data query volume and protocol deviation risk.

Why timeline risk is economically material.
- Direct operating costs accrue daily, and Tufts CSDD estimates Phase III direct daily costs at 55,716.00 USD and Phase II at 23,737.00 USD in 2023 dollars [38](https://csdd.tufts.edu/sites/default/files/2025-02/Aug2024%20Day%20of%20Delay%20White%20Paper%20Final.pdf).
- Opportunity cost can dominate direct costs, and Tufts CSDD estimates a day of delayed or lost prescription sales at approximately 800,000.00 USD in 2023 dollars [38](https://csdd.tufts.edu/sites/default/files/2025-02/Aug2024%20Day%20of%20Delay%20White%20Paper%20Final.pdf).

Resource allocation shifts that sponsors commonly underestimate.
- Clinical operations staffing: More site management capacity is needed for training, monitoring, and issue resolution across a larger and more heterogeneous site network.
- Data management and biostatistics: More subgroup focused monitoring and analysis planning is needed to ensure interpretable evidence for underrepresented populations.
- Medical monitoring and safety: Broader eligibility and more comorbidity variation can increase safety signal triage workload, requiring stronger monitoring plans consistent with FDA recommendations on inclusive eligibility and safe participation [23](https://www.fda.gov/media/190162/download).

Operational complexity drivers specific to inclusive recruitment.
- Multi language execution: Translation, interpreter scheduling, and multilingual call center operations increase coordination complexity and can create bottlenecks if not planned.
- Community engagement cadence: Sustained engagement requires ongoing events, feedback loops, and trust building, which are difficult to compress late in enrollment.
- Decentralized and hybrid components: Home health, telemedicine, and remote data capture can reduce participant burden but add vendor oversight and technology validation work.

Risk controls that reduce the probability that diversity efforts create net delay.
- Front load feasibility using real world data to test eligibility criteria and site catchment diversity, consistent with industry recommendations to use real world data for feasibility and recruitment planning [39](https://ctti-clinicaltrials.org/about/ctti-projects/real-world-data/ctti-recommendations-use-of-real-world-data-to-plan-eligibility-criteria-and-enhance-recruitment/).
- Use staged site activation with clear performance triggers to avoid uncontrolled site proliferation.
- Implement quality by design controls for new site types, focusing on training, data quality, and protocol adherence.

### 5.3. Comparative budgeting challenges for small versus large sponsors

Sponsor size strongly shapes the affordability of inclusive recruitment because many diversity enabling costs are fixed or semi fixed and require specialized capabilities.

Large sponsor budgeting advantages.
- Scale economies: Centralized recruitment teams, standing vendor master service agreements, and reusable multilingual materials reduce marginal cost per study.
- Portfolio smoothing: Large pipelines can justify permanent community engagement infrastructure and can spread technology investments across multiple programs.
- Negotiating leverage: Larger sponsors can negotiate better rates for home health, translation, and patient support services.

Small sponsor budgeting constraints.
- Higher unit costs: One time investments in Diversity Action Plan processes, community partnerships, and multilingual operations are harder to amortize across a small portfolio.
- Cash flow sensitivity: Small sponsors are more exposed to short term burn rate increases and may prioritize speed to proof of concept over broader representativeness.
- Limited operational depth: Fewer internal specialists increases reliance on CROs and niche vendors, raising oversight burden and the risk of misaligned incentives.

## 6. Current State of Clinical Trial Diversity

Across North American drug development programs intended for United States approval, demographic representativeness remains uneven as of March 10, 2026, with the most transparent and consistently published annual benchmarks coming from FDA CDER Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Reports. These reports show wide variability by therapeutic area and by individual program, reflecting both disease epidemiology and enrollment economics such as site footprint, inclusion and exclusion criteria, and the feasibility of recruiting in under resourced communities (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report 2024 PDF) [40](https://www.fda.gov/media/187276/download).

Key cross cutting observations relevant to government officials.
- Variability is the defining feature: In 2024 pivotal programs supporting novel drug approvals, female participation ranged from 9% to 98%, Hispanic or Latino participation ranged from 0% to 46%, and participants aged 65 years and older ranged from 1% to 97% depending on therapeutic area and program design (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report 2024 PDF) [40](https://www.fda.gov/media/187276/download).
- Race and ethnicity patterns are not uniform across therapeutic areas: In 2024, cancers programs averaged high Asian enrollment relative to other areas, while Black enrollment was described as the lowest enrolled category across therapeutic areas with a range from 0% to 68% (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report 2024 PDF) [40](https://www.fda.gov/media/187276/download).
- American Indian or Alaska Native participation remains very low and is often not emphasized in summary tables due to historically being less than 1% to 2% or not available, which limits statistical power and policy visibility for this population (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report 2024 PDF) [40](https://www.fda.gov/media/187276/download).
- Multinational trial conduct complicates United States representativeness: FDA notes that pivotal trials are commonly multinational and that some therapeutic areas enroll a high share of participants outside the United States, which can reduce the effective United States subgroup sample sizes needed for generalizability to North American populations (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report 2024 PDF) [40](https://www.fda.gov/media/187276/download).

Interpretation for this report.
- The current state is best characterized as improved transparency with persistent enrollment gaps, where the binding Diversity Action Plan era is expected to shift planning earlier but has not yet produced multi year post implementation outcomes as of March 10, 2026.
- Government levers that reduce participant burden and expand community based site capacity are directly aligned with the observed drivers of variability, particularly for older adults, rural residents, and lower income populations.

### 6.2. Gaps in representation by therapeutic area, geography, and socioeconomic status

Therapeutic area gaps.
- Oncology and other specialty areas show distinct enrollment profiles: FDA reported that in 2024 cancers programs averaged 44.0% Asian participation, illustrating that some therapeutic areas can skew heavily toward specific demographic groups depending on where trials are conducted and where disease specific recruitment networks exist (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report 2024 PDF) [40](https://www.fda.gov/media/187276/download).
- Older adult representation can be misaligned with disease burden: FDA noted that only 36.0% of participants in 2024 cancers programs were aged 65 years and older, despite many cancers disproportionately affecting older adults, suggesting a persistent evidence gap for geriatric oncology decision making (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report 2024 PDF) [40](https://www.fda.gov/media/187276/download).
- Device evidence can have additional demographic visibility gaps: A peer reviewed analysis of high risk cardiovascular device pivotal studies approved 2014 to 2022 reported substantial non reporting of race and ethnicity and very limited reporting of older adult participation, which can compound representativeness issues even before enrollment is assessed (Reporting of demographics and subgroup analyses in premarketing studies of FDA approved high risk cardiovascular devices, 2014 to 2022) [44](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11067925/).

Geography and site footprint gaps.
- Multinational enrollment can dilute United States subgroup sample sizes: FDA reported that pivotal trials were mostly multinational and highlighted therapeutic areas with high enrollment outside the United States, which can reduce the number of United States participants available for subgroup analyses relevant to North American policy and payer decisions (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report 2024 PDF) [40](https://www.fda.gov/media/187276/download).
- Within the United States, underrepresentation is often linked to where trial sites are located: Peer reviewed work using ClinicalTrials.gov and other sources has repeatedly associated representativeness gaps with site concentration in large academic centers and limited penetration into rural and safety net settings, which are more likely to serve lower income and underinsured populations (Race and ethnicity reporting and representation in US clinical trials) [45](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9302767/).

Socioeconomic status gaps.
- Socioeconomic status is not consistently captured as a standardized variable in FDA public demographic summaries, creating a measurement gap that can mask enrollment inequities by income, education, housing stability, and insurance status.
- Practical implication for officials: When socioeconomic variables are not routinely measured, policy evaluation must rely on proxies such as site type, geography, and participant burden metrics, or require enhanced data collection through public funding conditions and reporting requirements.

Populations with persistent low visibility.
- American Indian or Alaska Native participants: FDA states this category has historically been less than 1% to 2% or not available, limiting both representativeness and the feasibility of subgroup analyses (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report 2024 PDF) [40](https://www.fda.gov/media/187276/download).
- Hispanic or Latino participants: FDA reports a 0.0% to 46.0% range across therapeutic areas in 2024, indicating that some programs achieve meaningful inclusion while others remain near zero (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report 2024 PDF) [40](https://www.fda.gov/media/187276/download).

### 6.3. Barriers to participation and enrollment disparities in current trials

Barrier categories most associated with enrollment disparities.
- Financial and time costs: Lost wages, hourly work inflexibility, childcare needs, and transportation costs disproportionately affect lower income participants and contribute to higher screen failure and dropout rates when not offset by participant support services.
- Geographic access: Concentration of sites in major metropolitan academic centers limits participation for rural residents and for communities distant from specialty care hubs, reinforcing regional enrollment skews (Race and ethnicity reporting and representation in US clinical trials) [45](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9302767/).
- Eligibility criteria and comorbidity exclusions: Overly restrictive inclusion and exclusion criteria can systematically exclude populations with higher comorbidity burdens, including older adults and some racial and ethnic minority groups, reducing external validity.
- Trust and historical harms: Mistrust of medical research, concerns about exploitation, and fear of immigration or employment consequences can reduce willingness to enroll, particularly when community engagement is weak.
- Language and cultural fit: Limited availability of translated materials, bilingual staff, and culturally adapted consent processes reduces enrollment and retention for participants with limited English proficiency.
- Digital divide: Technology enabled recruitment and decentralized elements can improve access for some, but can also exclude participants without reliable broadband, smartphones, or digital literacy.

Enrollment process points where disparities commonly emerge.
- Awareness stage: Underrepresented communities may have lower exposure to trial opportunities due to limited outreach through trusted local channels.
- Screening stage: Higher rates of ineligibility can occur when protocols exclude common comorbidities or require extensive baseline testing.
- Retention stage: Visit frequency, travel distance, and complex procedures increase dropout risk, especially for participants with unstable transportation or caregiving responsibilities.

Evidence and reporting limitations that affect barrier diagnosis.
- ClinicalTrials.gov results reporting has improved over time, but race and ethnicity reporting completeness and consistency remain variable, which complicates real time monitoring of disparities and the evaluation of interventions (Race and ethnicity reporting and representation in US clinical trials) [45](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9302767/) (Racial and Ethnic Diversity in Clinical Studies Reported to ClinicalTrials.gov, 2009 to 2024) [46](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12330421/).

Operational implications for government officials.
- The most actionable barrier levers for public policy are those that reduce participant burden and expand community based research capacity, including transportation support, childcare support, paid time off models, and funding for safety net and rural site infrastructure.
- Monitoring should prioritize therapeutic areas where FDA reports low older adult enrollment or near zero Hispanic or Latino enrollment in some programs, because these patterns indicate where evidence generalizability risks are most likely to persist without targeted interventions (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report 2024 PDF) [40](https://www.fda.gov/media/187276/download).

### 7.1. Pharmaceutical and biotech strategies to meet diversity expectations

Sponsors are operationalizing diversity expectations as a protocol and program design requirement rather than a late stage recruitment campaign, aligning with FDA recommendations that Diversity Action Plans specify enrollment goals and measures to achieve them [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).

Common sponsor strategies observed across large pharma and emerging biotech include.
- Protocol design simplification: Reducing avoidable exclusion criteria, minimizing visit burden, and using pragmatic endpoints where appropriate to expand eligibility while preserving safety and interpretability, consistent with FDA and ecosystem recommendations to optimize trial design for inclusion [50](https://acrpnet.org/2024/08/15/diverse-inclusive-clinical-research-begins-with-optimized-trial-design).
- Portfolio level governance: Establishing cross functional diversity governance, role specific training, and routine review of diversity metrics as part of quality management, consistent with CTTI recommendations emphasizing long term organizational infrastructure rather than reactive study level tactics [51](https://ctti-clinicaltrials.org/access/ctti-recommendations-increasing-diversity-in-clinical-trials/).
- Data driven feasibility and site selection: Using EHR informed feasibility, claims and epidemiology inputs, and site performance analytics to select geographies and sites with access to underrepresented populations, including community based settings and nontraditional care touchpoints [50](https://acrpnet.org/2024/08/15/diverse-inclusive-clinical-research-begins-with-optimized-trial-design).
- Community and HCP mediated recruitment: Building sustained relationships with community clinicians and trusted messengers, and investing in pre trial education to improve awareness and reduce mistrust barriers [51](https://ctti-clinicaltrials.org/access/ctti-recommendations-increasing-diversity-in-clinical-trials/).
- Participant support budgets: Funding transportation, language services, flexible scheduling, and other supports to reduce economic and logistical barriers, then tracking utilization as a retention and equity metric [50](https://acrpnet.org/2024/08/15/diverse-inclusive-clinical-research-begins-with-optimized-trial-design).
- Decentralized and hybrid execution: Expanding home health, telemedicine, and local lab options to reduce travel burden, often paired with centralized monitoring and risk based quality controls to manage operational complexity [52](https://www.transceleratebiopharmainc.com/assets/personalized-clinical-trials-framework/).

Implementation implications for regulators and payers.
- Sponsors increasingly treat diversity deliverables as auditable artifacts, including documented rationale for enrollment goals, site strategy, and contingency actions when enrollment diverges from targets, which can improve transparency but also increases documentation and vendor oversight load [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).

### 7.2. CRO-led solutions and diversity-focused trial design services

CROs are positioning diversity execution as an integrated service line spanning feasibility, protocol design, site enablement, recruitment operations, and reporting, effectively productizing compliance support for sponsors with limited internal infrastructure.

Key CRO led solution patterns include.
- Diversity focused feasibility packages: Combining epidemiology, site access indicators, and operational constraints to propose enrollment scenarios and site mixes that better match intended use populations, then translating these into sponsor ready Diversity Action Plan inputs [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Site capacity building: Training and resourcing community sites to meet sponsor expectations for data quality, staffing, and regulatory readiness, reducing the tradeoff between representativeness and execution risk [53](https://www.acrohealth.org/di-grants/).
- Decentralized clinical trial enablement: Providing technology and operational consulting for hybrid and decentralized models, including change management and data flow mapping resources that support consistent execution across countries and sites [52](https://www.transceleratebiopharmainc.com/assets/personalized-clinical-trials-framework/).
- Patient engagement and inclusion services: Structured approaches to patient advisory input, culturally appropriate materials, and community outreach partnerships to improve recruitment and retention among underserved groups [54](https://www.parexel.com/solutions/integrated-clinical-development/patient-inclusion).
- Sponsor embedded staffing models: Functional service provider models that place diversity focused roles within sponsor teams to manage DAP related tracking, site performance, and corrective actions, reflecting the operationalization of diversity as a continuous process rather than a one time plan [55](https://www.iconplc.com/insights/blog/2024/06/26/leveraging-healthcare-intelligence-increase-diversity-oncology-clinical).

Operational risk considerations for government stakeholders.
- CRO consolidation of diversity services can improve standardization and speed, but it can also concentrate market power and raise concerns about smaller sponsor access to high quality diversity enablement, reinforcing the need for public sector capacity building and shared infrastructure in parallel sections of this report [56](https://www.acrohealth.org/2025/01/09/acro-announces-recipients-of-diversity-inclusion-site-resource-grants/).

### 7.3. Collaborative industry initiatives and shared resource models

Industry collaboration is increasingly used to reduce duplicative investments in tools, training, and community engagement, while creating reusable assets that can be adopted across competing sponsors.

Prominent collaboration models include.
- Precompetitive toolkits and shared guidance: Consortia publish aggregated solutions and implementation resources for improving participant diversity, enabling sponsors to adopt common approaches to site engagement, capacity building, and inclusive operations [47](https://www.transceleratebiopharmainc.com/assets/diversity-of-participants-2/).
- Cross stakeholder convening: Multi stakeholder initiatives bring together sponsors, sites, patient groups, and regulators to align on practical methods for setting enrollment goals and implementing them, including oncology focused benchmark discussions hosted by FDA with external partners [57](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/fda-meetings-conferences-and-workshops/benchmarks-diversity-oncology-clinical-trials-fda-acs-hybrid-symposium-10162024).
- Shared site enablement funding: Industry associations support grants and resource programs aimed at improving the readiness of sites that can reach underrepresented communities, helping address the capacity bottleneck that limits diverse enrollment even when sponsor intent is strong [53](https://www.acrohealth.org/di-grants/).
- Common protocol and operational assets: Reuse oriented initiatives develop templates and operational standards that can reduce protocol complexity and improve consistency, indirectly supporting diversity by lowering participant burden and site workload [58](https://www.appliedclinicaltrialsonline.com/view/transcelerate-digital-momentum-global-collaboration-2025-annual-report).

Observed impacts on compliance and innovation.
- Compliance enablement: Shared assets reduce the marginal cost of building DAP aligned processes, particularly for mid size sponsors that lack dedicated health equity teams [51](https://ctti-clinicaltrials.org/access/ctti-recommendations-increasing-diversity-in-clinical-trials/).
- Innovation diffusion: Precompetitive collaboration accelerates adoption of decentralized and hybrid methods and standardized community engagement practices, improving the speed at which effective approaches spread across the sponsor ecosystem [52](https://www.transceleratebiopharmainc.com/assets/personalized-clinical-trials-framework/).

### 7.4. Emerging business models addressing diversity and inclusion in trials

Diversity mandates are catalyzing new commercial models that monetize access, enablement, and measurement of representativeness, shifting diversity from a corporate responsibility theme to a contracted deliverable.

Emerging models include.
- Community site network aggregators: Organizations build or curate networks of community based sites and clinicians serving underrepresented populations, offering sponsors faster access to diverse participants with standardized training and operational support [59](https://www.ppd.com/news-item/thermo-fisher-scientific-and-national-minority-quality-forum-collaborate-to-make-clinical-research-more-accessible-to-historically-underserved-communities/).
- Diversity focused recruitment and engagement vendors: Specialized vendors provide culturally tailored outreach, multilingual materials, and community partnerships as managed services, often integrated with CRO recruitment operations [54](https://www.parexel.com/solutions/integrated-clinical-development/patient-inclusion).
- Data and analytics products for representativeness: Vendors offer feasibility and monitoring analytics that track enrollment composition against targets and disease epidemiology, enabling earlier corrective actions and more defensible documentation for regulators [50](https://acrpnet.org/2024/08/15/diverse-inclusive-clinical-research-begins-with-optimized-trial-design).
- Decentralized trial platform bundling: Technology providers and CROs bundle telehealth, eConsent, home nursing, and logistics into turnkey offerings that reduce travel burden and expand geographic reach, supporting inclusion when paired with community engagement and site capacity building [48](https://www.iqvia.com/newsroom/2025/03/iqvia-recognized-as-a-leader-in-idc-marketscapes-2024-assessment).
- Workforce and site enablement services: Training and mentorship programs target coordinator and investigator readiness at sites serving underserved communities, addressing a structural constraint that limits diverse enrollment even when recruitment demand exists [53](https://www.acrohealth.org/di-grants/).

Economic and policy relevance.
- These models can reduce sponsor execution risk by converting fixed internal build costs into variable contracted services, but they can also increase per patient costs and create dependency on a limited set of vendors, reinforcing the need for government supported infrastructure and standards discussed in later sections [51](https://ctti-clinicaltrials.org/access/ctti-recommendations-increasing-diversity-in-clinical-trials/).

## 8. Government Incentive Programs

Government incentives for clinical trial diversity in North America function primarily as capacity building and cost sharing mechanisms rather than direct rewards for meeting FDA diversity targets. In practice, the most scalable public levers are upstream investments that reduce the marginal cost of enrolling underrepresented participants, including community engagement infrastructure, recruitment and retention toolkits, and funding conditions that require credible accrual plans and progress reporting.

Across the United States, the incentive landscape is currently shaped by two countervailing forces.
- Expansion of formal FDA expectations for prospective diversity planning through Diversity Action Plans for certain studies under FDORA, which increases sponsor demand for recruitment infrastructure and community partnerships [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Volatility in federal research funding for some equity related programs and awards, which increases execution risk for academic and community based recruitment capacity that sponsors often rely on [60](https://apnews.com/article/32b9b7bad01457a5412af26e394e3735).

For government officials, the policy implication is that incentives should be evaluated on two dimensions.
- Whether they measurably reduce sponsor time and cost to reach representative enrollment.
- Whether they build durable community facing research capacity that persists beyond a single trial or therapeutic area.

Key effectiveness indicators for incentive programs.
- Time to site activation in community settings.
- Screening to enrollment conversion rates in underrepresented groups.
- Retention and visit adherence rates.
- Data quality and protocol deviation rates at newly activated sites.
- Sponsor reported incremental cost per enrolled participant by subgroup.

### 8.1. Federal funding, grants, and incentives supporting diverse trial enrollment

Federal support most often reaches diverse enrollment indirectly by funding the institutions, networks, and community partnerships that make inclusive recruitment operationally feasible. These mechanisms are most relevant to FDA diversity mandates when they reduce the incremental cost of adding representative United States sites, lowering participant burden, and improving trust and awareness.

Primary United States federal mechanisms that support diverse enrollment capacity.
- NIH Community Engagement Alliance: A national community engaged research network created to work with communities and community based organizations on trustworthy outreach and engagement practices, with regional teams and a consultative resource that provides tailored consultations to research teams [61](https://ceal.nih.gov/impact/effective-community-engagement-methodology-from-start).
- NCATS CTSA Program and Trial Innovation Network: Provides recruitment and retention resources and community engagement capabilities across the CTSA consortium, supporting multi site studies and enabling sponsors and investigators to access standardized toolkits and engagement support [62](https://ncats.nih.gov/sites/default/files/2024-03/NCATS_FY-2025_CJ.pdf).
- NIH HEAL Initiative recruitment policy: Uses funding stewardship requirements tied to accrual reporting and achievement expectations, creating a de facto incentive to design feasible recruitment plans and maintain enrollment performance to sustain award continuation [63](https://heal.nih.gov/funding/clinical-trials-policy).
- NIH RADx Underserved Populations: A large NIH investment focused on underserved and vulnerable populations, with a consortium scale footprint and documented project coverage across multiple underserved groups, which strengthened community recruitment infrastructure during and after the COVID 19 period [64](https://www.nih.gov/radx/events/radx-funding-opportunity-announcements-foa-frequently-asked-questions).

How these programs function as incentives for sponsors.
- Cost offsets: Federally funded networks reduce sponsor borne costs for community engagement design, recruitment materials, and local partnership development by providing reusable assets and trained personnel embedded in communities [65](https://ncats.nih.gov/research/research-activities/ctsa/projects/community-engagement).
- Risk reduction: Programs that condition continuation on accrual performance create operational discipline around recruitment feasibility, which can improve predictability for sponsors collaborating with NIH funded sites [63](https://heal.nih.gov/funding/clinical-trials-policy).
- Faster learning cycles: Large consortia generate implementation knowledge about what works in underserved settings, which can be translated into sponsor playbooks for future trials.

Practical takeaway for officials.
- The most effective federal incentives are those that pay for durable recruitment infrastructure and community partnerships that sponsors can reuse across multiple trials, rather than one time payments tied to a single protocol.

### 8.2. Public–private partnerships and state-level initiatives promoting inclusion

Public private partnerships are a central delivery model for inclusion because they combine sponsor operational capacity with public sector trust assets and community networks. In the United States, many of the most influential inclusion partnerships are structured as multi stakeholder consortia that publish tools, convene stakeholders, and standardize engagement practices.

High leverage partnership models.
- FDA and multi stakeholder convenings: FDA has co sponsored convenings focused on benchmarks and methods for diversity planning in oncology, supporting shared approaches to representativeness targets and operationalization across sponsors and sites [57](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/fda-meetings-conferences-and-workshops/benchmarks-diversity-oncology-clinical-trials-fda-acs-hybrid-symposium-10162024).
- CTSA hub based community engagement studios: CTSA affiliated institutions operate community engagement recruitment and retention studios that connect investigators and sponsors to community partners and recruitment expertise, functioning as a local implementation layer for national infrastructure [69](https://ctsi.mcw.edu/investigators/services/community-engagement-recruitment-retention-studio).
- PCORI Engagement Awards as partnership catalysts: PCORI funds engagement activities that can include recruitment and enrollment support for clinical trials, enabling partnerships among patients, community organizations, and research teams to improve feasibility and trust [70](https://www.pcori.org/funding-opportunities/announcement/engagement-award-convening-support-fall-2025-cycle).

State and regional initiatives.
- Regional CEAL initiatives: CEAL regional teams and affiliated initiatives support locally tailored engagement and pilot funding, which can be leveraged by sponsors seeking to activate community based sites and improve recruitment in underrepresented populations [71](https://ceal.nih.gov/programs).

Operational features of successful inclusion partnerships.
- Shared governance: Community partners participate in protocol feasibility review, recruitment messaging, and burden reduction decisions.
- Shared assets: Centralized translation, culturally adapted materials, and standardized training reduce duplication across sponsors.
- Data feedback loops: Rapid reporting on screening, enrollment, and retention by subgroup enables midcourse correction.

Outcome measurement guidance for officials.
- Require partnership funded programs to report a minimum dataset that includes enrollment and retention by race, ethnicity, age, sex, geography, and language preference, aligned to FDA demographic reporting expectations.
- Track time to first participant enrolled at community sites and compare to academic center baselines to quantify whether partnerships reduce activation friction.

Implementation risk to monitor.
- Public reporting indicates that federal funding volatility can disrupt community based research capacity, which can weaken public private partnership continuity and reduce sponsor willingness to rely on these channels for pivotal trials [60](https://apnews.com/article/32b9b7bad01457a5412af26e394e3735).

### 8.3. Regulatory incentives or flexibility for sponsors meeting diversity goals

Regulatory incentives for diversity are currently more limited than funding based incentives, but several FDA mechanisms can indirectly reward sponsors that invest in representative United States enrollment and credible diversity planning.

FDA mechanisms that can function as incentives.
- Diversity Action Plan waiver process: FDA guidance describes criteria and a process for sponsors to request waivers from the requirement to submit a Diversity Action Plan for certain studies, which functions as a form of regulatory flexibility when justified, although it is not a reward for strong performance [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Priority Review designation: FDA Priority Review shortens the review goal from 10 months to 6 months for qualifying applications, which can create a strong economic incentive for sponsors, but it is not specifically tied to diversity performance [72](https://www.fda.gov/patients/fast-track-breakthrough-therapy-accelerated-approval-priority-review/priority-review).
- Commissioner’s National Priority Voucher pilot program: FDA describes this pilot as accelerating review time to 1 to 2 months following submission for selected applications, and FDA has indicated that examples of qualifying efforts could include clinical trials that maintain robust United States enrollment to support generalizability for Americans, which aligns with the operational goals of diversity mandates when underrepresented United States populations are included in that robust enrollment [73](https://www.fda.gov/CNPV).

How officials should interpret regulatory incentives in economic terms.
- Review acceleration is economically meaningful because it can reduce time to decision and potentially time to market, but it is not guaranteed and is not designed as a diversity reward.
- The most realistic near term regulatory incentive pathway is to formalize predictable, transparent criteria where robust representativeness and credible Diversity Action Plan execution can be considered as a positive factor in discretionary programs, while avoiding any implication that safety or efficacy standards are relaxed.

Guardrails for any future diversity linked regulatory incentives.
- Maintain evidentiary standards: Incentives should not reduce requirements for adequate and well controlled evidence.
- Prevent perverse incentives: Avoid incentives that encourage superficial demographic quotas without retention, data quality, and subgroup analytic credibility.
- Require auditable metrics: Tie any flexibility to verified enrollment and retention outcomes and to documented burden reduction measures.

North American coordination note.
- Canada’s primary levers are funding and system capacity building rather than FDA style review acceleration, so cross border alignment is more feasible through shared metrics, shared community engagement standards, and interoperable recruitment infrastructure than through harmonized regulatory incentives.

## 9. Technology Solutions for Diverse Recruitment

Technology is increasingly treated as an execution layer for Diversity Action Plans and broader FDA expectations on representative enrollment, rather than a standalone recruitment tactic. As of March 10, 2026, the most operationally relevant technology patterns in North America cluster into four areas: digital outreach and participant enablement, analytics for inclusive site and cohort planning, decentralized and hybrid trial operations, and cost governance that ties technology spend to measurable enrollment and retention outcomes.

Across these areas, government stakeholders should evaluate technology solutions using three public interest lenses.
- Equity and access: Whether the tool reduces geographic, language, disability, and time burden barriers that disproportionately affect underrepresented populations.
- Evidence quality: Whether the tool improves representativeness without increasing protocol deviations, missing data, or differential dropout that can bias subgroup estimates.
- Accountability: Whether the tool produces auditable metrics aligned to Diversity Action Plan commitments and supports transparent reporting.

FDA guidance finalized in December 2025 explicitly recommends approaches spanning eligibility criteria, enrollment practices, and trial designs to enhance participation, which in practice increases demand for technology enabled recruitment and lower burden participation workflows [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial). FDA final guidance on conducting clinical trials with decentralized elements, issued in September 2024, provides a compliance oriented framework for remote and local trial activities that can expand access when implemented with appropriate oversight [74](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/conducting-clinical-trials-decentralized-elements).

### 9.1. Digital platforms and outreach tools targeting underrepresented populations

Digital outreach for diverse recruitment is shifting from broad advertising to targeted, community anchored, language accessible engagement that reduces friction from awareness to screening to consent.

High impact digital outreach patterns.
- Community integrated digital referral: Digital referral workflows embedded in community health centers, safety net clinics, and local pharmacies to convert routine care touchpoints into trial awareness and pre screening.
- Multichannel communication: SMS, WhatsApp style messaging, email, and voice outreach with culturally tailored scripts and rapid handoff to human navigators.
- Language accessible participant journeys: Translated recruitment materials, eConsent, and participant support content designed for limited English proficiency populations, aligned with federal language access expectations for covered entities and telehealth contexts [75](https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/ocr-dcl-section-1557-language-access.pdf).
- Accessibility by design: Mobile first interfaces, low bandwidth options, and disability accessible digital experiences to avoid excluding participants who rely on assistive technologies.

Public sector relevant infrastructure enablers.
- ClinicalTrials.gov modernization: The modernized ClinicalTrials.gov experience and updated API improve discoverability and data access for trial search and downstream tools that match patients to trials, supporting broader awareness and third party navigation services [76](https://clinicaltrials.gov/about-site/modernization).
- Privacy and trust controls: Recruitment platforms that use consumer facing digital channels must manage health data carefully, especially when data flows fall outside HIPAA. The FTC Health Breach Notification Rule updates and related guidance highlight breach notification and privacy expectations for many health apps and connected device ecosystems, which is directly relevant when recruitment relies on apps, trackers, or in app messaging [77](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/blog/2024/04/updated-ftc-health-breach-notification-rule-puts-new-provisions-place-protect-users-health-apps).

Operational risks and mitigations for diverse recruitment.
- Risk: Digital only outreach can widen the digital divide and under recruit rural, older, and low income populations.
- Mitigation: Pair digital outreach with community based navigators, paper and phone options, and local enrollment events.
- Risk: Algorithmic targeting can unintentionally exclude protected groups.
- Mitigation: Require documented inclusion criteria for outreach audiences, periodic bias audits, and human review of targeting rules.

Implementation guidance for sponsors and regulators.
- Require sponsors to document channel mix, language coverage, accessibility testing, and conversion metrics from impression to contact to consent to randomization.
- Encourage use of trusted messengers and community partners, with digital tools supporting scheduling, transportation coordination, and participant support rather than replacing human engagement.
- Align digital communications with the December 2025 FDA participation guidance emphasis on enrollment practices and trial designs that reduce burden and improve representativeness [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).

### 9.2. AI-driven analytics and predictive modeling for inclusive site selection

AI and predictive analytics are increasingly used to shift diversity from a late stage recruitment scramble to an early design and feasibility discipline. The most policy relevant use cases are inclusive site selection, enrollment forecasting by subgroup, and adaptive recruitment resource allocation.

Core analytic use cases.
- Inclusive site selection: Models that combine epidemiology, claims and EHR signals, social vulnerability proxies, travel time, and historical site performance to identify sites likely to enroll underrepresented populations while maintaining quality.
- Enrollment forecasting by subgroup: Predictive models that estimate screen fail and randomization rates by demographic strata, enabling realistic Diversity Action Plan targets and contingency planning.
- Recruitment resource optimization: Algorithms that reallocate outreach spend and navigator capacity across sites to reduce representativeness gaps during enrollment.

Fairness and governance requirements.
- Data representativeness: Training data often reflect historical under enrollment, which can cause models to recommend the same non diverse sites.
- Proxy risk: Socioeconomic and geographic proxies can correlate with protected characteristics, creating discrimination risk if not governed.
- Auditability: Government reviewers should expect model documentation, feature rationale, bias testing, and monitoring plans.

Regulatory and civil rights alignment.
- The 2024 HHS Section 1557 final rule and related OCR communications emphasize nondiscrimination obligations, including in telehealth and clinical algorithms contexts, which is relevant when AI tools influence who is reached, screened, or supported [78](https://www.hhs.gov/civil-rights/for-individuals/disability/guidance-on-nondiscrimination-in-telehealth/index.html).
- FDA diversity expectations increasingly emphasize early planning and site selection choices as part of achieving representative enrollment, reinforced by the December 2025 FDA guidance on enhancing participation [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).

Practical procurement criteria for government funded or government partnered trials.
- Require explainability artifacts suitable for IRBs and regulators, not only data science teams.
- Require performance reporting by subgroup, including false negative rates for eligibility matching and differential outreach response.
- Require privacy preserving architectures when using EHR and claims data, including role based access, minimization, and retention limits.

Evidence base signals.
- Peer reviewed methods research continues to develop fairness aware site selection and recruitment allocation approaches, indicating feasibility of improving diversity without sacrificing enrollment performance when fairness constraints are explicitly modeled [79](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.19407).

### 9.3. Decentralized and hybrid trial models enhancing access and diversity

Decentralized and hybrid models expand access by moving trial activities closer to participants through telemedicine, local labs, home health visits, direct to patient shipping, and remote data capture. These models are most effective for diversity when they are designed to reduce travel and time burden while preserving clinical oversight and data integrity.

Regulatory foundation.
- FDA final guidance on conducting clinical trials with decentralized elements, issued September 2024, defines decentralized elements and provides recommendations for operational control, safety oversight, and data handling across nontraditional locations [74](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/conducting-clinical-trials-decentralized-elements).
- FDA communications on decentralized trials emphasize that regulatory requirements remain the same as traditional trials, while decentralized elements can include local labs and telemedicine visits that reduce participant burden [80](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-takes-additional-steps-advance-decentralized-clinical-trials).

Diversity mechanisms enabled by decentralized and hybrid designs.
- Geographic reach: Remote visits and local testing reduce the need to live near academic medical centers, supporting rural and underserved urban participation.
- Time flexibility: Evening and weekend telehealth visits and asynchronous ePRO collection reduce work and caregiving conflicts.
- Participant support integration: Technology can coordinate transportation for required in person visits, home nursing, and multilingual support.

Operational constraints that can undermine diversity if unmanaged.
- Digital divide: Participants without reliable broadband, smartphones, or digital literacy may be excluded.
- Differential missingness: Wearables and apps can increase missing data in groups with lower device comfort or unstable connectivity.
- Vendor fragmentation: Multiple decentralized vendors can increase burden on sites and participants if workflows are not integrated.

Implementation practices that preserve inclusion.
- Offer modality choice: Allow participants to select in person, hybrid, or remote options when scientifically appropriate.
- Provide devices and connectivity support: Loaner devices, data plans, and technical support reduce exclusion.
- Standardize training and escalation: Home health staff and local labs require consistent training, documentation, and safety escalation pathways.

Evidence signals on diversity and performance.
- Industry backed analyses reported by Tufts CSDD PACT consortium partners indicate decentralized approaches are associated with improved participation across multiple underrepresented demographics, supporting the policy rationale for hybridization when aligned to protocol needs [81](https://www.fiercebiotech.com/cro/decentralized-trials-include-more-diverse-patients-traditional-trials-says-industry-backed).

### 9.4. Cost–benefit considerations of technological recruitment solutions

Cost benefit evaluation should treat technology as a portfolio of interventions that shift both direct trial costs and the probability distribution of timeline outcomes. For diversity mandates, the dominant economic value often comes from reducing enrollment delays and improving retention, rather than reducing per participant costs.

Cost categories that matter most for diverse recruitment technology.
- Fixed costs: Platform licensing, systems integration, cybersecurity, validation, and staff training.
- Variable costs: Per participant telehealth visits, home nursing, device provisioning, translation and interpretation, and participant support services.
- Quality costs: Monitoring, data reconciliation across vendors, and remediation of missing data or protocol deviations.

Benefit categories that matter most for government and sponsors.
- Enrollment acceleration: Faster identification and screening of eligible participants through digital prescreening and EHR enabled matching.
- Retention improvement: Reduced dropout through lower burden visits, reminders, and participant support coordination.
- Representativeness improvement: Higher likelihood of meeting Diversity Action Plan targets, reducing regulatory and reputational risk.
- Evidence generalizability: Better subgroup evidence can reduce postmarketing evidence burdens and improve real world effectiveness decisions.

Decision framework for selecting technology solutions.
- Fit to protocol: Use decentralized elements where endpoints and safety monitoring allow remote or local execution under FDA decentralized guidance [74](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/conducting-clinical-trials-decentralized-elements).
- Fit to population: Prioritize tools that reduce travel, time, language, and disability barriers for the intended use population.
- Fit to compliance: Ensure privacy and breach response readiness for app based recruitment and remote data capture, consistent with FTC guidance for health apps and connected devices [77](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/blog/2024/04/updated-ftc-health-breach-notification-rule-puts-new-provisions-place-protect-users-health-apps).

Procurement and oversight recommendations for government officials.
- Require measurable KPIs: Enrollment rate by subgroup, time to first participant, screen fail by subgroup, dropout by subgroup, and missing data rates by subgroup.
- Require equity safeguards: Language access coverage, accessibility testing, and non digital alternatives.
- Require audit trails: Documented workflows and data lineage to support FDA inspection readiness and public accountability.

Economic interpretation aligned to FDA participation expectations.
- FDA December 2025 guidance emphasizes designing eligibility, enrollment practices, and trial designs to enhance participation, which implies that technology spend is most defensible when it is tied to protocol level burden reduction and demonstrable representativeness outcomes rather than generic marketing reach [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).

Practical cost control levers.
- Standardize vendor stacks across programs to reduce integration and training costs.
- Use hybrid models to concentrate high cost in person assessments on a subset of visits while decentralizing routine follow up.
- Build reusable multilingual content libraries and eConsent templates to reduce per study translation costs while maintaining quality review.

## 10. Economic Impact Analysis

This section translates Diversity Action Plan execution and broader FDA expectations into economic terms that matter to North American public sector decision makers and trial sponsors. The central economic tension is that diversity execution increases near term operating costs and can raise timeline risk, while also reducing downstream costs associated with non generalizable evidence, postmarketing evidence obligations, avoidable safety signals, and inequitable real world effectiveness.

Across phases, the most material economic exposure is typically not the direct cost of inclusive tactics but the cost of delay when enrollment slows or sites underperform. Phase III direct cost per day has been estimated at $55,716.00 and Phase II at $23,737.00 in 2023 USD, implying that even modest schedule slippage can dominate the incremental spend on participant support and community based recruitment infrastructure (Tufts CSDD white paper PDF at [38](https://csdd.tufts.edu/sites/default/files/2025-02/Aug2024%20Day%20of%20Delay%20White%20Paper%20Final.pdf)).

Economic evaluation should therefore be framed as a portfolio of risk managed investments.
- Direct costs: Added line items for inclusive recruitment, retention, language access, and data operations.
- Indirect costs: Higher probability of enrollment delays, protocol amendments, and site remediation.
- Avoided costs: Reduced likelihood of regulatory friction, postmarketing study requirements, and label limitations driven by subgroup uncertainty.
- System value: Better targeting and effectiveness across populations, reducing wasteful utilization and disparities driven by trial evidence gaps.

### 10.1. Short‑term cost increases by trial phase and therapeutic area

Short term cost increases are driven by four mechanisms that vary by phase.
- Higher fixed startup costs: More sites, more diverse site types, more contracts, more training, and more monitoring.
- Higher variable per participant costs: Transportation, childcare, translation, community health worker support, and higher touch retention.
- Higher screening and retention intensity: Broader outreach increases screening volume, and under resourced sites may require sponsor funded enablement.
- Higher data operations burden: More granular demographic capture and subgroup analysis planning, aligned with FDA expectations for enrollment practices and trial design (FDA guidance December 2025 at [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial)).

Phase sensitivity.
- Phase I and early Phase II: Diversity tactics are often constrained by safety and intensive visit schedules, so the cost impact is concentrated in participant support and eligibility modernization rather than large site expansion.
- Late Phase II and Phase III: Cost pressure is highest because pivotal evidence must be generalizable and because enrollment scale makes site network decisions and recruitment throughput economically decisive.

Therapeutic area sensitivity.
- Oncology and rare disease: Higher baseline per patient costs and narrower eligibility can make representative enrollment more expensive, especially when biomarker testing and complex procedures are required.
- Cardiometabolic and infectious disease: Larger addressable populations can reduce per participant recruitment cost, but geographic and socioeconomic access barriers can still drive added spend for community based sites.

### 10.2. Long‑term economic benefits of representative data and improved outcomes

Representative data creates long term economic value through three channels.
- Higher evidence generalizability: Reduced uncertainty about benefit risk across intended use populations, lowering the probability of post approval evidence generation to resolve subgroup questions.
- Better dosing and safety management across populations: Earlier detection of subgroup differences can prevent costly safety signals, label restrictions, and avoidable adverse event burden.
- Stronger real world effectiveness and adherence: When trial populations reflect real patients, clinical practice adoption is more predictable and payer coverage decisions face fewer evidence gaps.

Evidence quality and regulatory efficiency.
- FDA Drug Trials Snapshots continues to highlight demographic participation and subgroup analyses for CDER approvals, reinforcing that representativeness is a visible component of regulatory credibility and public accountability (FDA Drug Trials Snapshots portal at [20](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-approvals-and-databases/drug-trials-snapshots)).
- Persistent underrepresentation of Black and Hispanic participants has been documented in analyses of FDA Drug Trials Snapshots data, implying ongoing risk that non representative evidence will require additional studies or limit confidence in subgroup performance (analysis of 2017 to 2023 approvals at [43](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12680696/)).

Economic benefits for sponsors.
- Reduced probability of late stage protocol amendments: Inclusive eligibility and recruitment planning earlier in development reduces expensive mid trial redesign.
- Reduced commercial friction: Better subgroup evidence can reduce payer skepticism and support value based contracting where outcomes are stratified.
- Reduced litigation and reputational risk: Stronger subgroup evidence reduces the risk of claims that safety or effectiveness was not adequately characterized for affected populations.

Economic benefits for governments.
- Better targeting of public health interventions: Representative evidence improves guideline quality and reduces trial to practice translation gaps.
- Improved equity outcomes: Better evidence for historically underrepresented groups supports more effective allocation of public resources and reduces avoidable disparities.

### 10.3. Healthcare system savings from more effective, inclusive treatments

Healthcare system savings are realized when inclusive evidence reduces ineffective utilization and prevents avoidable adverse outcomes in populations that were previously evidence poor. The savings mechanism is strongest when underrepresentation correlates with different baseline risk, comorbidity patterns, pharmacogenomic variation, or access barriers that affect adherence and follow up.

System level context for North America.
- US national health spending was estimated at $5,300,000,000,000.00 in 2024, with growth projected to average 5.6% per year for 2024 to 2033, indicating that even small percentage improvements in effectiveness and safety can translate into large absolute savings (CMS projections summary PDF at [84](https://www.cms.gov/files/document/nhe-projections-forecast-summary.pdf)).

Where savings are most plausible.
- Reduced adverse drug events and downstream care: Better subgroup safety characterization can reduce emergency visits and hospitalizations from preventable toxicity.
- Reduced trial and error prescribing: More representative efficacy data can reduce cycling through ineffective therapies, especially in chronic disease.
- Reduced disparities driven by evidence gaps: When evidence is weak for specific populations, clinicians may under prescribe effective therapies or over rely on less effective alternatives.

Policy relevant measurement approach.
- Track post approval utilization and outcomes by race, ethnicity, age, sex, and geography, aligned with the demographic categories used in FDA transparency reporting.
- Use payer claims and EHR linked registries to quantify avoidable utilization attributable to subgroup uncertainty.
- Prioritize therapeutic areas with high spend and high disparity burden for diversity investments, because the potential savings pool is larger.

### 10.4. Market access advantages of diverse trial data

Diverse trial data can improve market access by reducing evidence objections from regulators, HTA bodies, and payers, and by supporting labeling and clinical guideline adoption across broader populations.

Regulatory and payer facing advantages.
- Reduced risk of label limitations: When subgroup evidence is weak, sponsors may face conservative labeling, additional warnings, or postmarketing requirements that delay full commercial uptake.
- Stronger payer confidence: Payers increasingly scrutinize external validity, especially when real world populations differ from trial populations in comorbidity and social risk.
- Improved global acceptability: Multinational programs seeking alignment across jurisdictions benefit when enrollment reflects the intended use populations in major markets.

North American convergence signals.
- The United States has moved from guidance based expectations toward mandated Diversity Action Plans for certain studies under FDORA, with FDA providing detailed recommendations on eligibility, enrollment practices, and trial designs in its December 2025 guidance (FDA guidance page at [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial)).
- Canada has issued guidance describing expectations for a voluntary SGBA Plus Demographics Action Plan in clinical trial applications, signaling a policy direction that can increase the market value of inclusive evidence for sponsors operating across North America (Health Canada guidance at [85](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/programs/consultation-modernizing-framework-clinical-trials/draft-guidance-document-sponsors-sgba-plus-demographics-action-plan-applications.html)).

Operational implication for sponsors and governments.
- Sponsors should treat diverse enrollment as a market access asset and document it as part of evidence strategy, not only as compliance.
- Governments can improve access outcomes by aligning procurement, formulary, and reimbursement evidence expectations with representativeness, while offering targeted support to reduce the incremental cost burden at under resourced sites.

## 11. Balancing Regulatory Requirements with Economic Realities

Balancing diversity mandates with enrollment economics requires treating representativeness as a design and governance variable rather than a late stage recruitment add on. The most economically resilient approaches combine early protocol choices that reduce avoidable exclusions, operational models that shift fixed costs into shared infrastructure, and regulatory engagement that enables proportionate, risk based execution.

Across North America, the practical balancing point is to align three elements.
- Regulatory intent: Ensure evidence is generalizable to the intended use population through eligibility, enrollment practices, and trial design choices that enhance participation and reduce burden [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Sponsor economics: Minimize timeline risk and avoid late protocol amendments by locking diversity relevant design decisions before pivotal activation.
- Public sector role: Use targeted cost sharing and infrastructure investments to reduce marginal cost of reaching underrepresented populations without weakening evidence quality.

The strategies below are designed for government officials to operationalize this balance through procurement, grant design, and regulatory coordination, while preserving sponsor accountability for Diversity Action Plan execution where required under FDORA and related FDA guidance processes [86](https://www.congress.gov/committee-report/117th-congress/house-report/348/1).

### 11.1. Cost‑sharing and incentive models between government and industry

Cost sharing is most effective when it funds durable capacity that multiple sponsors can use, rather than reimbursing one off recruitment campaigns. Government can reduce sponsor marginal costs by underwriting community based research infrastructure, data enablement, and participant support mechanisms that are otherwise duplicated across trials.

High leverage cost sharing models.
- Shared site enablement grants: Public funding to equip community health centers and regional hospitals with research staffing, training, and quality systems so they can run regulated trials reliably, lowering sponsor startup and monitoring burden.
- Network based recruitment infrastructure: Government supported engagement networks that provide trusted messengers, culturally competent materials, and referral pathways that sponsors can contract with under standardized terms, reducing vendor fragmentation.
- Participant cost offsets: Public programs that reimburse travel, lodging, childcare, and time off work for eligible participants, reducing sponsor direct payments and improving retention while maintaining ethical safeguards.
- Data and interoperability subsidies: Public investment in EHR based feasibility and referral tools that improve identification of eligible participants and reduce screen failure costs.

Existing public infrastructure that can be adapted for cost sharing.
- NIH community engagement infrastructure: CEAL provides community engaged practices and consultative resources that can be leveraged to improve outreach and trust building for research participation [87](https://ceal.nih.gov/).
- NIH CTSA ecosystem: The CTSA program supports community engagement and includes the Trial Innovation Network, which can be used to accelerate multi site recruitment and retention planning [65](https://ncats.nih.gov/research/research-activities/ctsa/projects/community-engagement).
- BARDA clinical studies infrastructure: BARDA supports clinical studies through its Clinical Studies Network model, demonstrating a federal approach to contracting for trial infrastructure and operational expertise that can be extended to access focused site expansion [88](https://www.medicalcountermeasures.gov/barda/dcd).
- PCORI engagement awards: PCORI funds engagement and capacity building activities that can strengthen stakeholder partnerships relevant to recruitment and retention in patient centered research [89](https://www.pcori.org/funding-opportunities).

Implementation guardrails for government officials.

### 11.2. Phased implementation strategies to mitigate economic burden

Phased implementation reduces economic shock by sequencing the highest return actions first, then scaling infrastructure and geographic reach as evidence and operational maturity increase. The goal is to reduce late stage amendments and timeline slips by front loading design decisions while staging operational expansion.

A practical phased framework aligned to development stages.
- Phase 0 feasibility and protocol design: Use real world data and site feasibility to set realistic enrollment goals, minimize avoidable exclusions, and pre specify subgroup relevant endpoints and analyses.
- Early clinical phases: Pilot inclusive recruitment tactics in a limited number of community sites to validate operational workflows, translation needs, and participant support budgets.
- Pivotal phase: Scale proven tactics, expand site geography, and deploy decentralized elements where appropriate to reduce participant burden.
- Post approval commitments when needed: If certain populations remain difficult to enroll in pivotal trials, plan targeted postmarketing evidence generation while maintaining transparency and scientific justification.

Operational sequencing that reduces cost and risk.
- Start with eligibility criteria modernization: Broadening eligibility where scientifically justified can increase the eligible pool without proportional increases in recruitment spend, consistent with FDA guidance on enhancing participation [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Add decentralized elements selectively: Use telehealth visits, local labs, and home health where they reduce burden and improve retention, consistent with FDA guidance on conducting trials with decentralized elements [74](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/conducting-clinical-trials-decentralized-elements).
- Expand sites after process control: Scale to additional community sites only after monitoring findings, protocol deviation rates, and data completeness meet predefined thresholds.

Government role in enabling phased adoption.
- Provide phased grants: Fund readiness and pilot execution first, then release scale up funds based on performance.
- Offer shared services: Centralized training, translation libraries, and quality management support reduce duplication across sponsors.
- Standardize reporting: Require consistent demographic funnel metrics so sponsors can compare performance across phases and adjust early.

Phasing success metrics that balance compliance and economics.
- Time to first participant enrolled at new community sites.
- Screen failure rate by key demographic strata.
- Retention and visit completion rates for decentralized versus site based participants.
- Monitoring finding rates and corrective action closure time.

Key risk to manage.
- Phasing must not become deferral: Regulators and funders should require that pivotal programs incorporate validated inclusion tactics rather than postponing representativeness to post approval evidence generation.

### 11.3. Regulatory flexibility for smaller sponsors and adaptive approaches

Smaller sponsors face higher fixed cost sensitivity and less negotiating power with CROs and site networks. Regulatory flexibility should therefore focus on proportionate compliance pathways that preserve scientific validity while reducing unnecessary administrative burden.

Flexibility mechanisms already embedded in the FDORA Diversity Action Plan framework.
- Waivers and partial waivers: FDORA contemplates waiver pathways tied to FDA guidance on criteria and process for evaluating waiver requests, which is particularly relevant for rare diseases and constrained populations [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies). [86](https://www.congress.gov/committee-report/117th-congress/house-report/348/1).
- Timing alignment with key meetings: FDA has recommended that diversity planning be discussed in the context of trial design feedback interactions, supporting earlier course correction and reducing late rework costs [90](https://www.fda.gov/media/179593/download).

Adaptive compliance approaches suitable for smaller sponsors.
- Proportionate targets with justification: Set enrollment goals based on epidemiology, feasibility, and disease prevalence, documenting constraints and mitigation steps.
- Hybrid evidence strategies: Combine smaller pivotal trials with targeted postmarketing studies or registries for underrepresented groups when pivotal enrollment is infeasible, with clear scientific rationale.
- Platform and master protocol participation: Join shared infrastructure trials where feasible to spread fixed costs across multiple assets.
- Use decentralized elements to reduce site footprint: Smaller sponsors can reduce the number of high cost academic sites by using decentralized elements for follow up and routine assessments, consistent with FDA decentralized elements guidance [74](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/conducting-clinical-trials-decentralized-elements).

Regulatory adjustments government officials can support without lowering standards.
- Standard templates and tooling: Provide public templates for diversity planning, demographic reporting, and community engagement documentation to reduce legal and consulting spend.
- Pre competitive training and certification: Fund standardized training for community sites and investigators so small sponsors can onboard sites faster.
- Centralized ethics and contracting support: Shared IRB reliance models and standardized contract clauses reduce startup time and legal costs.

Quality and compliance safeguards.
- Require auditable rationale for any reduced scope approach: Smaller sponsor flexibility should be tied to documented feasibility constraints and a plan to close evidence gaps.
- Monitor data quality at new sites: Under resourced sites can increase deviation risk, so flexibility should be paired with training and monitoring support.

### 11.4. International harmonization and alignment of diversity expectations

International alignment reduces duplicative requirements and enables sponsors to design multiregional programs that satisfy multiple regulators with one coherent representativeness strategy. For North America, harmonization is increasingly relevant because pivotal evidence often comes from multiregional clinical trials.

Key harmonization anchors.
- ICH E17 multiregional clinical trials: E17 provides general principles for planning and designing multiregional confirmatory trials, supporting consistent regional representation planning and analysis across jurisdictions [91](https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/ich-guideline-e17-general-principles-planning-design-multi-regional-clinical-trials-scientific-guideline).
- ICH E6 R3 good clinical practice modernization: E6 R3 emphasizes quality by design and proportionate risk based approaches, which can be used to integrate diversity execution into critical to quality planning rather than treating it as an add on cost center [92](https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/ich-e6-good-clinical-practice-scientific-guideline).
- Canada movement toward demographics planning: Health Canada published draft guidance in December 2025 on SGBA Plus and a demographics action plan concept for clinical trial applications, signaling convergence toward prospective planning expectations in North America [85](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/programs/consultation-modernizing-framework-clinical-trials/draft-guidance-document-sponsors-sgba-plus-demographics-action-plan-applications.html).

Practical alignment strategies for sponsors and regulators.
- Common core demographics dataset: Align race, ethnicity, sex, age group, and geography reporting definitions across US and Canada to reduce rework and improve comparability.
- Joint scientific advice: Encourage parallel consultations with FDA and Health Canada for pivotal programs to reconcile representativeness expectations early.
- Mutual recognition of decentralized operational controls: Use FDA decentralized elements guidance as a baseline for cross border operational controls, while mapping to local privacy and licensure rules [74](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/conducting-clinical-trials-decentralized-elements).

Economic benefits of harmonization.
- Reduced duplication: One multiregional enrollment strategy reduces the need for separate region specific protocol amendments.
- Faster activation: Standardized site readiness and monitoring expectations reduce vendor and CRO customization.
- Lower evidence gap risk: Better planned regional representation reduces the likelihood of post approval evidence obligations driven by generalizability concerns.

Policy actions for North American officials.
- Establish a North America representativeness working group: Coordinate FDA, Health Canada, and relevant public funders on definitions, reporting, and acceptable mitigation strategies.
- Publish crosswalk guidance: Provide a mapping between FDA enhancing participation guidance and Health Canada demographics action plan expectations to reduce compliance ambiguity [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial). [85](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/programs/consultation-modernizing-framework-clinical-trials/draft-guidance-document-sponsors-sgba-plus-demographics-action-plan-applications.html).
- Incentivize multiregional designs that include underserved North American geographies: Use grants and procurement to expand trial access beyond major academic hubs while maintaining GCP quality controls.

## 12. Policy Recommendations for Government Officials

This section translates FDA diversity mandates and the observed enrollment economics into actionable policy options for North American government officials. Recommendations prioritize measurable inclusion outcomes, predictable compliance pathways, and cost containment by shifting a portion of fixed diversity enablement costs from individual sponsors to shared public infrastructure, while preserving sponsor accountability for execution and data quality.

Across all recommendations, officials should apply three operating principles.
- Predictability: Provide clear expectations, templates, and review timelines for Diversity Action Plans and inclusive protocol design to reduce rework and delay risk.
- Proportionality: Scale requirements and supports to trial phase, disease context, and sponsor size, while maintaining minimum representativeness standards for pivotal evidence.
- Verifiability: Tie incentives and flexibilities to auditable metrics, including enrollment, retention, and data completeness, aligned with FDA guidance on enhancing participation and FDORA Diversity Action Plan expectations [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).

### 12.1. Evidence‑based policy levers to encourage compliance and inclusion

Recommended policy levers that increase compliance while limiting sponsor cost volatility.

- Make Diversity Action Plan quality a procurement and funding gate: Require a scored Diversity Action Plan and inclusive protocol plan for eligibility in federal and provincial or state funded clinical research, public private partnerships, and government facilitated trial networks, using FDA guidance elements as scoring criteria such as eligibility justification, enrollment practices, and burden reduction tactics [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Use milestone based payments for inclusion outcomes: For government funded trials and contracts, structure payments around verified milestones such as site activation in underserved areas, enrollment of underrepresented groups, and retention through primary endpoint, rather than only activity based payments.
- Offer targeted tax credits or refundable credits for participant support costs: Create credits for direct participant enablement expenses that reduce access barriers, including transportation, lodging, childcare, translation, and mobile nursing, with caps and audit requirements to prevent cost shifting.
- Create a fast lane for sponsors that demonstrate repeatable inclusion performance: Provide prioritized meeting scheduling and predictable review touchpoints for sponsors with a documented track record of meeting Diversity Action Plan goals and maintaining data quality, while preserving FDA independence on regulatory decisions.
- Expand public recognition with standardized metrics: Publish a government maintained dashboard that summarizes representativeness for trials supporting approvals, leveraging FDA Drug Trials Snapshots as a transparency anchor and adding consistent measures of US and Canada participation and subgroup completeness [20](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-approvals-and-databases/drug-trials-snapshots).
- Align incentives with decentralized and hybrid execution where appropriate: Provide grants or reimbursement add ons for validated decentralized elements that reduce travel burden, consistent with FDA final guidance on conducting clinical trials with decentralized elements [93](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-roundup-september-17-2024).

### 12.2. Regulatory reform opportunities to reduce economic strain on sponsors

Reforms that reduce avoidable cost drivers while maintaining the core representativeness objective.

- Standardize Diversity Action Plan submission artifacts across product centers: Provide a single harmonized template, controlled vocabulary, and minimum dataset for Diversity Action Plans to reduce sponsor rework across drugs, biologics, and devices, consistent with FDORA driven Diversity Action Plan expectations described by FDA guidance materials [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Create a predictable waiver and modification pathway: Publish objective criteria for when a Diversity Action Plan goal may be modified due to epidemiology, feasibility, or safety constraints, and require a mitigation plan such as additional sites, extended recruitment windows, or postmarketing evidence generation.
- Expand use of master protocols and shared control arms where appropriate: Encourage platform and umbrella designs in areas with multiple competing trials to reduce duplicative recruitment pressure on the same communities, lowering per sponsor fixed costs.
- Reduce administrative friction for community based and decentralized execution: Clarify expectations for local healthcare provider involvement, remote assessments, and data flow responsibilities to reduce contracting and monitoring overhead, aligned with FDA final guidance on decentralized elements [93](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-roundup-september-17-2024).

## 13. ROI Analysis of Diversity Implementation

This section quantifies the return on investment from diversity implementation as an economic decision under FDORA era expectations, not only a compliance activity. The core ROI mechanism is risk reduction in timelines and evidence generalizability, where modest incremental operating costs can be outweighed by avoided delay costs and avoided postmarketing evidence obligations.

ROI should be evaluated at portfolio level and program level using a consistent model that separates direct trial operating costs from commercial opportunity costs, and that explicitly prices the probability of delay and the probability of additional evidence generation triggered by nonrepresentative data. Updated empirical estimates for the value of a delay day and direct daily trial costs materially change ROI thresholds compared with older industry heuristics, making smaller improvements in cycle time economically meaningful when applied across multiple programs [38](https://csdd.tufts.edu/sites/default/files/2025-02/Aug2024%20Day%20of%20Delay%20White%20Paper%20Final.pdf).

### 13.1. Economic modeling of return on investment in diversity strategies

Recommended modeling approach for government and sponsor decision makers.
- Model type: Decision analytic expected value model with scenario analysis and sensitivity analysis.
- Unit of analysis: Clinical program, with roll up to portfolio.
- Time horizon: From pivotal study start through 3 years post approval to capture postmarketing evidence and label expansion effects.
- Perspective: Sponsor financial perspective plus public sector perspective for cost offsets and health outcomes.

Core ROI equation for a diversity investment package.
- Net benefit (USD): Avoided delay cost plus avoided incremental evidence cost plus avoided remediation cost minus incremental diversity enablement cost.
- ROI (%): Net benefit divided by incremental diversity enablement cost.
- Break even condition: Incremental diversity enablement cost less than or equal to expected avoided costs.

Key parameters that should be explicitly priced.
- Delay day value: Use updated empirical estimates rather than legacy blockbuster era assumptions.
- Direct daily trial cost: Use phase specific daily cost estimates to price operational extensions.
- Probability of delay: Baseline probability and reduction attributable to diversity enablement.
- Probability of additional evidence: Probability of postmarketing requirement or additional trial due to generalizability concerns.
- Recruitment and retention effects: Changes in screen failure, enrollment rate, and dropout.

How to use these values in an expected value ROI calculation.
- Expected avoided commercial loss (USD): Delay days avoided multiplied by delay day value multiplied by probability that the diversity strategy prevents the delay.
- Expected avoided operational cost (USD): Direct daily cost multiplied by trial days avoided.
- Expected avoided remediation cost (USD): Probability of remediation multiplied by remediation cost, where remediation includes protocol amendments, additional sites, additional recruitment waves, and potential postmarketing evidence.

Practical ROI thresholds implied by updated delay day values.
- If a diversity strategy reduces expected delay by 10.0 days, the expected avoided sales loss is 5,000,000.00 USD using the 500,000.00 USD per day estimate, before considering avoided operational costs[98](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38773058/) .
- If the same strategy reduces Phase III duration by 10.0 days, the expected avoided direct operating cost is 557,160.00 USD using the Phase III daily cost estimate[38](https://csdd.tufts.edu/sites/default/files/2025-02/Aug2024%20Day%20of%20Delay%20White%20Paper%20Final.pdf) .

Model governance recommendations for North American public sector use.
- Require sponsors receiving public incentives to submit a standardized ROI worksheet with assumptions, including delay day value, direct daily cost, and a documented basis for assumed delay reduction.
- Require sensitivity analysis on at least 3 parameters: delay days avoided, probability of delay reduction, and incremental diversity cost.
- Use a common discount rate and time horizon across programs to enable comparability in public reporting.

### 13.2. Public health value derived from representative clinical data

Public health value channels that can be monetized or evaluated as cost effectiveness.
- Improved external validity: Representative enrollment improves confidence that benefit risk findings generalize to the intended use population, reducing uncertainty in clinical decision making and coverage policy.
- Earlier detection of subgroup differences: Better powered subgroup analyses can identify differential safety signals or efficacy heterogeneity earlier, reducing avoidable adverse outcomes and ineffective treatment exposure.
- Reduced disparities in access to innovation: Inclusive trial operations can function as an access pathway for under served communities, particularly when trials provide diagnostics, monitoring, and specialist access.

Public sector valuation approaches.
- Cost utility analysis: Translate improved outcomes into quality adjusted life years and compare incremental cost per quality adjusted life year for diversity enablement investments.
- Budget impact analysis: Estimate changes in downstream utilization from fewer adverse events, fewer treatment failures, and improved adherence due to better matched therapies.
- Value of information: Quantify the economic value of reducing decision uncertainty for payers and guideline bodies when evidence is representative.

Evidence base and measurement constraints as of March 10, 2026.
- FDA Drug Trials Snapshots provides transparency on who participated in pivotal trials supporting novel drug approvals, enabling year over year monitoring of representativeness by sex, race, ethnicity, age, and US participation[20](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-approvals-and-databases/drug-trials-snapshots) .
- The 2024 Drug Trials Snapshots Summary Report was released by FDA CDER and summarizes demographic groups across novel drug approvals, supporting public accountability and trend analysis[99](https://content.govdelivery.com/accounts/USFDA/bulletins/3e6fa58) .

Operational public health metrics recommended for government dashboards.
- Representativeness index: Absolute difference between trial enrollment shares and disease burden shares for key demographic groups.
- Subgroup evidence completeness: Proportion of pivotal trials with analyzable subgroup outcomes for race and ethnicity categories used in FDA reporting.
- Access burden reduction: Proportion of participants using decentralized elements, participant support services, and local care options, stratified by geography and socioeconomic proxies.

Link to decentralized elements as an equity lever.
- FDA issued final guidance on conducting clinical trials with decentralized elements in September 2024, providing a regulatory basis for hybrid models that can reduce travel and time burdens that disproportionately affect underrepresented populations[93](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-roundup-september-17-2024) .

### 13.3. Cost‑effectiveness comparisons of different diversity approaches

Cost effectiveness should be compared using a consistent outcome metric.
- Primary sponsor metric: Cost per incremental enrolled participant from an underrepresented group who is retained through primary endpoint.
- Secondary sponsor metric: Cost per day of expected delay avoided.
- Public sector metric: Cost per quality adjusted life year gained attributable to improved representativeness and earlier subgroup signal detection.

Comparative assessment of common approaches.
- Eligibility modernization: Often high ROI because it reduces screen failures and expands the eligible pool without proportional increases in site count, but requires careful safety justification and monitoring.
- Community site enablement: Higher fixed costs for training, staffing, and quality systems, but can produce durable capacity that benefits multiple trials and sponsors.
- Participant support services: Transportation, childcare, and compensation administration can be cost effective when the binding constraint is visit burden rather than awareness.
- Decentralized and hybrid operations: Can reduce participant burden and improve retention, but requires investment in technology, vendor oversight, and data integrity controls.
- Targeted outreach and navigation: Can be cost effective when paired with trusted intermediaries and when referral pathways are measurable.

### 13.4. Long‑term market and societal benefits of inclusive research

Long term market benefits for sponsors and the North American innovation ecosystem.
- Reduced regulatory and payer uncertainty: Representative evidence can reduce the likelihood of additional evidence requests and can support clearer labeling and broader clinical adoption.
- Faster diffusion into real world practice: Clinicians are more likely to use therapies when trial populations resemble their patients, improving uptake and reducing off label uncertainty.
- Improved pharmacovigilance efficiency: Better baseline subgroup data improves signal detection and interpretation post approval.

Societal benefits relevant to government officials.
- Health equity: Inclusive trials can reduce disparities in evidence generation, which is a prerequisite for equitable guideline development and coverage decisions.
- Workforce and infrastructure development: Investments in community based research capacity create durable clinical research jobs and strengthen local health systems.
- Trust and participation flywheel: Repeated engagement with communities can increase willingness to participate in future research, lowering long run recruitment costs.

Strategic implication for North American policy design.
- Treat diversity enablement as shared infrastructure: Public investment in community site readiness, broadband access, and standardized demographic reporting can reduce duplicative sponsor spending while improving compliance.
- Use FDA transparency mechanisms as accountability anchors: Drug Trials Snapshots and annual summary reports provide a public baseline for monitoring progress and targeting incentives[20](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-approvals-and-databases/drug-trials-snapshots) .

Future facing ROI considerations.
- As decentralized elements become more standardized under FDA guidance, the marginal cost of hybrid participation options is expected to decline, improving ROI for inclusion strategies that reduce visit burden[93](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-roundup-september-17-2024) .

## 14. Future Outlook and Emerging Trends

This section projects how clinical trial diversity mandates are likely to evolve after 2025, with emphasis on the interaction between regulatory expectations, operational economics, and enabling technologies. The near term outlook is characterized by increasing formalization of diversity planning, tighter linkage between diversity evidence and submission quality, and broader use of decentralized and data driven recruitment methods to reduce participant burden and cost. Internationally, convergence is expected through ICH Good Clinical Practice modernization and expanding transparency infrastructure, which together increase pressure for consistent demographic planning and reporting across multinational pivotal programs.

### 14.1. Anticipated regulatory developments and guidance updates post‑2025

Regulatory trajectory in the United States is expected to move from guidance driven expectations toward more standardized, auditable implementation of Diversity Action Plans, with increasing emphasis on traceability from epidemiology to enrollment goals to operational execution.

Key developments likely after 2025.
- Standardization of Diversity Action Plan review expectations: FDA is likely to operationalize more consistent review practices across centers by using templates, checklists, and structured data elements aligned to the December 2025 guidance on eligibility criteria, enrollment practices, and trial designs, which already frames diversity as a design and conduct issue rather than a late recruitment activity [2](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/enhancing-diversity-clinical-trial-populations-eligibility-criteria-enrollment-practices-and-trial).
- Increased linkage to submission quality and inspection readiness: Sponsors should expect diversity planning artifacts to be treated more like quality system evidence, including documentation of rationale for exclusions, monitoring of enrollment against goals, and corrective actions when goals are missed, consistent with the design quality and risk management direction in ICH E8 R1 and the modernization of ICH E6 R3 [100](https://database.ich.org/sites/default/files/ICH_E6%28R3%29_EWG_WorkPlan_2025_0721_FINAL.pdf).
- Expansion of decentralized elements as an inclusion expectation: FDA finalized guidance on conducting clinical trials with decentralized elements in September 2024, and post 2025 interactions are expected to increasingly treat decentralized and hybrid approaches as a default option to reduce burden for underrepresented populations when scientifically appropriate [101](https://www.hhs.gov/guidance/document/conducting-clinical-trials-decentralized-elements).
- More explicit expectations for demographic data quality and granularity: FDA issued guidance on collection of race and ethnicity data in January 2024, and future updates are likely to emphasize consistent categorization, completeness, and transparency to support subgroup interpretability and public accountability [3](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/collection-race-and-ethnicity-data-clinical-trials-and-clinical-studies-fda-regulated-medical).

North American convergence signals.
- Canada: Health Canada published draft guidance in December 2025 describing expectations for voluntary submission of an SGBA Plus Demographics Action Plan in clinical trial applications, signaling a likely pathway toward more formalized demographic planning expectations in Canada over the next several years [85](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/programs/consultation-modernizing-framework-clinical-trials/draft-guidance-document-sponsors-sgba-plus-demographics-action-plan-applications.html).
- United States and Canada alignment opportunity: A plausible post 2025 trend is alignment of plan structure, demographic categories, and reporting cadence to reduce duplicative sponsor work for cross border programs, especially for multinational pivotal trials seeking both FDA and Health Canada outcomes.

Practical implications for sponsors and government officials.
- Sponsors: Expect higher value placed on early epidemiology justified enrollment goals, protocol level burden reduction, and auditable monitoring plans that show how enrollment will be corrected if it drifts.
- Government officials: Expect increased leverage from shared infrastructure investments, because regulators are signaling that inclusion is increasingly a design and operations requirement, not only a reporting requirement.

### 14.2. Technological innovations reducing costs and improving inclusion

The dominant technology trend post 2025 is the shift from point solutions for recruitment toward integrated inclusion operations that combine decentralized execution, EHR enabled feasibility, and automation of high friction coordinator tasks.

Emerging innovations with high inclusion and cost leverage.
- Modernized trial registries and transparency tooling: ClinicalTrials.gov modernization is adding new modules and results oriented structures, which can improve discoverability and reduce friction for participants and referring clinicians, especially when paired with plain language study documents and structured eligibility summaries [76](https://clinicaltrials.gov/about-site/modernization).
- Public trial mapping and proximity search: The EU CTIS trial map launched in March 2025 demonstrates a scalable model for helping patients find trials geographically, a concept that North American systems may emulate to reduce access barriers and improve site distribution planning [102](https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/human-regulatory-overview/research-development/clinical-trials-human-medicines/clinical-trials-information-system).
- AI assisted eligibility and matching with auditability: Research systems are increasingly focused on explainable, auditable AI for patient trial matching to reduce manual screening burden and expand the set of trials considered for each patient, which can improve inclusion when deployed across safety net health systems [103](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.08026).
- Eligibility criteria structuring and computability: Ontology driven representations of eligibility criteria are emerging to standardize and operationalize criteria across trials, enabling computable feasibility checks and reducing avoidable exclusions that disproportionately affect underrepresented populations [104](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.21724).
- Amendment prediction and protocol optimization: New methods that learn from historical protocol amendments can reduce costly mid trial changes, which often disrupt recruitment and retention and can disproportionately affect community sites with limited staffing [105](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.06300).

Inclusion economics mechanisms enabled by technology.
- Reduced participant burden: Televisits, eConsent, home health, and local lab networks reduce travel time and out of pocket costs, aligning with FDA decentralized guidance and improving retention for participants with inflexible work schedules or limited transportation [101](https://www.hhs.gov/guidance/document/conducting-clinical-trials-decentralized-elements).
- Reduced coordinator burden: Automated pre screening and structured eligibility extraction can reduce manual chart review time, enabling community sites to participate without proportionally increasing staffing.
- Better targeting and fewer screen failures: EHR based feasibility and matching can reduce screen failure rates, which is a major hidden cost driver when expanding to more diverse sites.

Implementation risks that will shape adoption.
- Digital divide: Hybrid modality choice remains necessary because technology only improves inclusion when participants can access devices, broadband, and language appropriate support.
- Algorithmic bias and governance: AI matching tools require bias testing, documentation, and human oversight to avoid systematically excluding groups with incomplete EHR data or different care patterns.

### 14.3. Projected adoption curves and compliance patterns across industry

Post 2025 adoption is expected to follow a two speed pattern, with large sponsors and large CRO networks standardizing Diversity Action Plan operations first, followed by smaller sponsors adopting through templates, shared vendors, and public infrastructure.

Expected compliance patterns through 2026 to 2030.
- Early majority behavior among large sponsors: Large sponsors are likely to embed diversity planning into governance gates, protocol authoring, and vendor selection, because the cost of delay and submission risk is high and because they can amortize fixed costs across portfolios.
- Template driven adoption among small and mid size sponsors: Smaller sponsors are likely to rely on CRO provided playbooks, standardized participant support packages, and decentralized vendors to meet expectations without building internal infrastructure.
- Therapeutic area stratification: Adoption will be fastest in areas with high competition for participants and high public scrutiny, such as oncology and cardiometabolic disease, where recruitment is already a dominant timeline risk.
- Operationalization through quality systems: ICH E6 R3 adoption at Step 4 in January 2025 signals a broader shift toward risk based quality management and fit for purpose processes, which will likely be used to justify and audit diversity execution as part of overall trial quality [106](https://mhir.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/ICH_E6R3_Step4_FinalGuideline_2025_0106.pdf).

Leading indicators government officials can monitor.
- Submission artifacts: Increasing consistency of Diversity Action Plan structure, epidemiology justification, and monitoring plans in submissions and meeting packages.
- Site network shifts: Growth in community based sites and hybrid models, with measurable reductions in travel burden and missed visit rates.
- Transparency outputs: Increased completeness and usability of demographic reporting in public facing systems, including FDA Drug Trials Snapshots and modernized ClinicalTrials.gov results modules [76](https://clinicaltrials.gov/about-site/modernization).

Adoption curve risks.
- Cost concentration risk: Without shared infrastructure, fixed costs of community site enablement and participant support may remain concentrated on individual programs, slowing adoption among smaller sponsors.
- Compliance formalism risk: Sponsors may meet documentation requirements without materially improving access if incentives reward plan completeness more than enrollment outcomes.

### 14.4. Global convergence of diversity standards and harmonization efforts

Global convergence is expected to accelerate through two channels: harmonized Good Clinical Practice modernization and expanding transparency infrastructure that makes demographic gaps more visible and politically salient.

Convergence drivers.
- ICH modernization: ICH E6 R3 reached Step 4 adoption in January 2025, and its implementation across regions is expected to reinforce risk based quality management and fit for purpose trial design, which supports consistent approaches to inclusive enrollment planning across multinational programs [106](https://mhir.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/ICH_E6R3_Step4_FinalGuideline_2025_0106.pdf).
- WHO equity guidance: WHO issued global guidance in September 2024 emphasizing more effective and equitable clinical trials and explicitly calling for improved diversity of trial participants, which supports policy diffusion beyond any single regulator [107](https://www.who.int/news/item/25-09-2024-new-global-guidance-puts-forward-recommendations-for-more-effective-and-equitable-clinical-trials).
- EU transparency infrastructure: The EU CTIS public portal and trial map launched in March 2025, and revised transparency rules applicable from June 2024, increase public visibility into where trials occur and can indirectly pressure sponsors to broaden geographic access and inclusion strategies [102](https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/human-regulatory-overview/research-development/clinical-trials-human-medicines/clinical-trials-information-system).

Likely harmonization outcomes relevant to North America.
- Converging expectations on representativeness: Even where explicit diversity mandates differ, regulators are converging on the principle that pivotal evidence should reflect the intended use population, increasing the likelihood that multinational programs will adopt a single global inclusion strategy rather than region specific approaches.
- Greater alignment on operational methods: Decentralized and hybrid methods are increasingly recognized across regulators as a way to reduce burden and improve access, supporting harmonized operational playbooks.
- Data comparability pressure: As transparency systems mature, sponsors will face pressure to use consistent demographic categories and reporting structures to enable cross trial comparisons, which can reduce duplicative reporting costs over time.

Constraints on full harmonization.
- Legal and cultural differences: Privacy regimes, anti discrimination laws, and local norms about collecting race and ethnicity data differ, limiting direct standardization of demographic variables.
- Infrastructure variability: Community site readiness and broadband access vary widely, so harmonized expectations may still require region specific enablement investments.

## 15. Strategic Implementation Roadmap

This roadmap operationalizes diversity mandates as a quality and evidence generalizability requirement, while explicitly managing enrollment economics through staged adoption, measurable performance, and shared infrastructure. It is designed for North American regulators and sponsors to align Diversity Action Plans with Good Clinical Practice modernization and emerging Canadian expectations for demographics planning in clinical trial applications.

### 15.1. Phased implementation recommendations for regulatory bodies and sponsors

Implementation principles.
- Risk based staging: Apply the most prescriptive requirements first to pivotal studies with high public health impact, high expected utilization, or known differential outcomes across subpopulations, while allowing proportionate approaches for rare diseases and small populations.
- Early integration: Require diversity planning at the same decision points where protocol feasibility and site strategy are set, so diversity targets are not treated as late stage recruitment fixes.
- Auditability by design: Align diversity planning documentation with modern GCP expectations for quality management and traceability, including ICH E6 R3 adoption by FDA and planned Health Canada implementation effective April 1, 2026 [108](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/drugs-health-products/drug-products/announcements/implementation-ich-e6r3-guideline-notice.html).

Recommended phased roadmap for regulators.
- Phase 0 readiness and standardization window: Publish standardized Diversity Action Plan templates, review checklists, and waiver decision criteria, and provide a public technical assistance channel for sponsors and IRBs.
- Phase 1 targeted enforcement for pivotal studies: Prioritize review of Diversity Action Plans for Phase 3 or other pivotal studies, focusing on whether enrollment goals are epidemiologically justified and operationally feasible, consistent with FDA draft guidance expectations for Diversity Action Plans [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Phase 2 expansion to earlier development and platform trials: Encourage diversity planning in Phase 2 and adaptive designs where feasible, emphasizing eligibility criteria modernization and decentralized components to reduce participant burden.
- Phase 3 cross border alignment: Coordinate with Health Canada on convergent expectations for demographics action plans in clinical trial applications, including acceptance of equivalent strategies previously submitted to other regulators when they meet Canadian expectations [85](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/programs/consultation-modernizing-framework-clinical-trials/draft-guidance-document-sponsors-sgba-plus-demographics-action-plan-applications.html).

Recommended phased roadmap for sponsors.
- Phase 0 portfolio triage: Classify programs by expected diversity risk, including therapeutic area disparities, geographic access constraints, and anticipated site footprint.
- Phase 1 protocol and feasibility redesign: Reduce avoidable exclusions, incorporate hybrid or decentralized visits where clinically appropriate, and pre specify subgroup analyses aligned to intended use.
- Phase 2 operational execution: Implement site selection that optimizes both enrollment and representativeness, deploy multilingual materials, and use retention supports such as transportation and flexible scheduling.
- Phase 3 evidence and transparency: Ensure consistent race and ethnicity data collection and reporting practices aligned with FDA draft guidance on race and ethnicity data collection and evolving federal standards [3](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/collection-race-and-ethnicity-data-clinical-trials-and-clinical-studies-fda-regulated-medical).

Time anchored compliance planning.
- FDA Diversity Action Plan requirements are statutory under FDORA and apply to certain clinical investigations for which enrollment commences 180 days after publication of the final FDA guidance, so sponsors should build internal readiness well before finalization to avoid protocol and site rework [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Health Canada has signaled formalization of demographics planning through draft guidance for sponsors and will implement ICH E6 R3 effective April 1, 2026, which increases the value of harmonized documentation and quality management across U.S. and Canada trials [108](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/drugs-health-products/drug-products/announcements/implementation-ich-e6r3-guideline-notice.html).

### 15.2. Stakeholder engagement strategies to maximize compliance and trust

Stakeholder map and engagement objectives.
- Patients and community organizations: Build trust, reduce perceived exploitation, and improve retention through co designed materials and feedback loops.
- Investigators and sites: Reduce coordinator burden and improve operational feasibility through standardized workflows, training, and realistic enrollment targets.
- IRBs and ethics boards: Align on culturally appropriate consent, language access, and participant support services.
- Regulators and payers: Demonstrate that diversity planning improves evidence generalizability and reduces post market uncertainty.

Trust building practices that directly affect enrollment economics.
- Community partnership agreements: Establish standing relationships with community health centers, faith based organizations, and patient advocacy groups, with clear expectations on outreach, privacy, and compensation.
- Transparency commitments: Publicly describe what demographic data will be collected, how it will be used, and how results will be shared back to communities.
- Participant burden reduction: Offer transportation, childcare stipends where permissible, flexible visit windows, and hybrid visit options to reduce dropout.
- Language access: Provide translated consent and study materials and interpreter support, and ensure readability testing for health literacy.

Operational governance for engagement.
- Sponsor level diversity governance: Create a cross functional committee spanning clinical operations, biostatistics, regulatory, medical, and procurement to approve Diversity Action Plans and monitor execution.
- Site enablement packages: Provide sites with recruitment toolkits, culturally tailored scripts, and standardized race and ethnicity data collection procedures aligned with FDA recommendations [3](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/collection-race-and-ethnicity-data-clinical-trials-and-clinical-studies-fda-regulated-medical).
- Regulator convenings: Regulators should host periodic public workshops with sponsors, CROs, IRBs, and community representatives to clarify expectations and share implementation lessons, reducing uncertainty costs.

Data stewardship and privacy safeguards.
- Minimize data friction: Collect only necessary demographic variables, document purpose limitation, and ensure secure handling consistent with applicable privacy laws.
- Address algorithmic bias: When using AI assisted recruitment or site selection, require bias testing, audit trails, and human oversight consistent with quality management expectations under modern GCP [109](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/e6r3-good-clinical-practice-gcp).

### 15.3. Metrics and frameworks for measuring progress and success

Measurement framework.
- Use a three layer model.
- Input metrics: Resources deployed to enable diversity.
- Process metrics: Whether recruitment and retention operations are functioning as designed.
- Outcome metrics: Whether enrolled and analyzed populations match justified targets and improve evidence quality.

Core metrics set for Diversity Action Plan execution.
- Representativeness attainment: Difference between planned enrollment distribution and actual enrolled distribution by race, ethnicity, sex, age, and geography.
- Screening yield equity: Screen failure rates by subgroup to detect eligibility criteria or operational barriers.
- Time to diversity target: Time from first patient in to achieving pre specified subgroup enrollment thresholds.
- Retention equity: Discontinuation and lost to follow up rates by subgroup.
- Data completeness: Percent of participants with complete race and ethnicity fields using standardized categories.

Evaluation and accountability mechanisms.
- Pre specified justification review: Regulators should assess whether enrollment goals are justified by disease epidemiology and intended use, consistent with FDA Diversity Action Plan draft guidance [1](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/diversity-action-plans-improve-enrollment-participants-underrepresented-populations-clinical-studies).
- Mid study corrective action triggers: Define thresholds that require protocol amendments, site additions, or enhanced participant support when gaps persist.
- Public transparency: Use ClinicalTrials.gov results reporting and FDA transparency initiatives to improve comparability and public trust, while acknowledging known inconsistencies in reporting across studies [46](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12330421/).

Success criteria beyond enrollment.
- Evidence quality: Demonstrate adequate subgroup sample sizes for clinically meaningful safety and efficacy interpretation where scientifically justified.
- Operational efficiency: Show that diversity improvements did not materially increase protocol deviations or data query rates, consistent with quality management under ICH E6 R3 [109](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/e6r3-good-clinical-practice-gcp).

### 15.4. Resource allocation guidance for agencies and trial sponsors

Resource allocation principles.
- Invest in shared infrastructure first: Centralized tools and services reduce marginal cost per trial, especially for smaller sponsors.
- Fund burden reduction interventions: Transportation, flexible visits, and decentralized components often improve both diversity and retention, reducing costly replacements.
- Prioritize data quality: Standardized demographic data collection and monitoring prevents downstream regulatory friction and reanalysis.

Agency level resource allocation.
- Standardized templates and review capacity: Build dedicated review teams and checklists for Diversity Action Plans, including waiver evaluation pathways.
- Public infrastructure investments: Support community based research networks, investigator diversity training, and multilingual recruitment resources.
- Harmonization workstreams: Coordinate U.S. and Canada expectations through joint technical guidance and mutual learning, leveraging Health Canada draft demographics action plan guidance and ICH E6 R3 implementation timelines [108](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/drugs-health-products/drug-products/announcements/implementation-ich-e6r3-guideline-notice.html).

Sponsor and CRO resource allocation.
- Budget categories that most directly move diversity outcomes.
- Site footprint optimization: Add high access sites such as community hospitals and federally qualified health center affiliated sites where feasible.
- Participant support services: Transportation, visit flexibility, and language services.
- Data systems: EDC and eConsent configurations that support standardized race and ethnicity capture and completeness monitoring.
- Workforce: Dedicated diversity operations leads and community liaisons embedded in study teams.

Procurement and contracting guidance.
- Performance based CRO contracting: Tie a portion of CRO fees to process metrics such as data completeness and retention equity, not only gross enrollment.
- Site contracts with equity supports: Include budget line items for community outreach, interpreter services, and extended hours.

Economic guardrails to prevent cost escalation.
- Avoid late stage rescue spending: Require early feasibility evidence that diversity targets are achievable with planned sites and supports.
- Use adaptive resource reallocation: Shift recruitment spend toward channels and geographies demonstrating higher yield for underrepresented groups, while monitoring for selection bias.
- Leverage transparency data: Use FDA Drug Trials Snapshots and other public sources to benchmark therapeutic area patterns and set realistic, justified targets [20](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-approvals-and-databases/drug-trials-snapshots).

## 17. Appendices: Economic Models and Data Tables

This appendix provides transparent, reusable economic model structures and quantitative tables that support the report’s enrollment economics and policy analyses. Models are presented as parameterized templates so agencies and sponsors can substitute program specific inputs, including therapeutic area, trial phase, baseline enrollment performance, and the incremental operational requirements associated with Diversity Action Plans and inclusive recruitment practices.

All models are designed to be auditable and to separate direct trial operating costs from time value impacts associated with enrollment delays. Where possible, parameters are anchored to publicly available regulator publications and peer reviewed analyses, and to sponsor benchmark research that reports daily direct trial costs and the economic value of time.

Key modeling conventions used throughout this appendix.
- Perspective: Sponsor financial perspective for direct costs and time value, with optional public sector overlays for incentive design.
- Time horizon: Trial execution through primary completion, with optional extension to approval and post approval evidence generation.
- Discounting: Use jurisdiction standard discount rates for public sector analyses and sponsor WACC for private sector analyses, with sensitivity ranges.
- Equity constraint: Diversity targets are treated as constraints or as objective function weights, depending on the policy scenario.
- Data governance: Demographic reporting assumptions align with ClinicalTrials.gov results reporting practices and FDA transparency initiatives.

### 17.1. Models and data tables

Economic model templates.

Model 1 Incremental cost of diversity implementation per trial.
- Purpose: Estimate incremental budget required to meet diversity enrollment targets relative to baseline recruitment operations.
- Core equation.
- Incremental cost (USD) = Fixed enablement cost (USD) + Variable recruitment cost per participant (USD) times Incremental participants recruited (count) + Retention support cost per participant (USD) times Total participants (count) + Decentralized enablement cost per participant (USD) times Participants using decentralized components (count).
- Recommended parameter blocks.
- Fixed enablement cost: Community partner contracting, translation and cultural adaptation, staff training, DAP governance and documentation.
- Variable recruitment cost: Additional outreach channels, mobile research units, travel support, childcare support, enhanced site activation in underserved geographies.
- Retention support: Visit burden reduction, participant navigation, technology support.
- Decentralized enablement: Devices, home health visits, telehealth coordination.
- Outputs.
- Incremental cost per randomized participant (USD).
- Incremental cost as percent of baseline trial budget (percent).
- Cost per incremental underrepresented participant enrolled (USD).

Model 2 Enrollment delay cost and time value model.
- Purpose: Quantify the economic impact of slower enrollment when diversity targets are binding and operational capacity is constrained.
- Core equation.
- Total delay cost (USD) = Delay days (days) times Direct trial cost per day (USD per day) + Delay days (days) times Value of lost sales per day (USD per day) times Probability of approval (unitless).
- Notes on parameterization.
- Direct trial cost per day can be phase specific.
- Value of lost sales per day should be scenario based and therapeutic area specific.
- Probability of approval can be applied as a risk adjustment when modeling expected value.
- Outputs.
- Break even delay days where diversity investment is cost neutral.
- Maximum allowable delay days under a fixed budget constraint.

Model 3 Cost effectiveness of recruitment interventions.
- Purpose: Compare alternative recruitment strategies on cost per incremental underrepresented participant and on time to target.
- Core equation.
- ICER style metric (USD per incremental participant) = Incremental cost of intervention (USD) divided by Incremental underrepresented participants enrolled (count).
- Complementary metric.
- Time gain (days) = Baseline time to target (days) minus Intervention time to target (days).
- Decision rule.
- Select interventions on the efficient frontier of cost per incremental participant and time gain.

Model 4 Sponsor compliance optimization under DAP constraints.
- Purpose: Allocate recruitment resources across sites and channels to meet DAP targets at minimum cost.
- Decision variables.
- Site level enrollment targets by demographic group.
- Budget allocation across channels such as community outreach, digital, referrals, mobile visits.
- Objective.
- Minimize total cost subject to enrollment targets, timeline constraints, and operational capacity.
- Constraints.
- Minimum enrollment by demographic group.
- Maximum enrollment per site per month.
- Maximum budget.
- Minimum data completeness for demographic reporting.

Model 5 Public sector incentive sizing model.
- Purpose: Size grants, tax credits, or infrastructure subsidies to offset incremental sponsor costs while preserving incentives for efficiency.
- Core equation.
- Incentive (USD) = Share of eligible incremental costs (percent) times Verified incremental costs (USD) capped at Maximum incentive (USD).
- Verification approach.
- Eligible costs tied to auditable line items such as translation, participant support, community partner payments, decentralized enablement.
- Performance conditions.
- Payment tranches linked to milestones such as first participant in, percent of target achieved, results reporting completeness.

Implementation notes for analysts.
